# The case for doing less for your community

The case for doing less for your community.

You've been told to show up for your community, but that's only half the job.

People ask me a lot about how we’ve built our community. Not just the scale of it (the 750,000 followers, the 600-plus paying members, the sold-out events) but the intimacy of it. The fact that it still feels, to a lot of people inside it, like something small and personal, despite six years of growth. And I never quite know how to answer that, because the honest answer is: I don’t think we ever approached it any other way.

One of the first things I did when I started PLM on Instagram, six years ago, was a story series called Marketer Monday. I’d DM people I’d spotted in our comments, or resharing our stuff, or just floating around in the same corner of the internet, and invite them to answer a few questions to be featured on our stories. It wasn’t a growth strategy, although it turned out to be a pretty good one. It just made sense to me. Why would I talk about our community when I could talk with them?

I think that instinct - to build with rather than for - is the thing that’s shaped everything we’ve done since. And I also think it’s the thing most community builders skip, not because they don’t care, but because nobody really talks about it.

“Just show up for your community” is rubbish advice.

The standard advice goes like this: show up consistently, over-deliver, be generous, give give give. And there’s truth in it - a neglected community is its own kind of failure, and generosity matters. But I think we’ve built an entire philosophy of community around one direction of travel, and it has a ceiling most people only discover once they’ve hit it.

The ceiling looks like this: you’ve built something people genuinely value. They show up, they engage, they might even pay for it. And somehow it still feels like it’s running entirely on your energy. Like the moment you stop, it stops. Like you’ve created something people love consuming but haven’t quite created something they love belonging to.

That’s because “for” creates a dynamic where you are the source and your community are the recipients. It’s generous, yes - but it keeps people at arm’s length from the thing they’re supposedly part of. They’re watching a world you’ve built, rather than living inside one they’ve helped shape and there’s only so long you can watch something before you start to wonder whether you actually belong there, or whether you’re just a very engaged spectator.

“With” is different: “with” is what happens when people stop being recipients and start being participants - when they have a role, when their contribution changes what the thing is, when they’d notice if they left not just because they’d miss the content, but because something would be missing without them.

Think about the communities you’ve felt most at home in... I’d bet it wasn’t because someone did an extraordinary job of delivering things to you, it’s because at some point you were part of what made it good.

This isn’t a new idea.

And the brands getting it right aren’t doing anything complicated - they’ve just made a different choice about who gets to be involved.

Refy is probably my favourite example of this: when they launched their concealer last year, they set up pop-ups in LA and London, invited real people in to get shade-matched, and those people became the campaign. Then when they launched their skin tint earlier this year, they'd built it with community testers from the start - shaped by the people who were going to buy it before it went on sale. Their own site describes it as "created with REFY community testers, shaped by your feedback." The product and the campaign, both built with. Not as a one-off idea, but as how they operate.

Then there’s Rhode (because what is a PLM piece without me gushing over the Rhode marketing team), who have a testing panel - customers who get unreleased products before anything goes public, try them, and feed back on what launches. By the time something hits their site, the people who'll buy it have already had a hand in making it. They announced their new masks earlier this year with "we tapped the Rhode testing panel" - not a focus group, not influencers, but their community.

Finally: Sprout Social have a Substack called Social Futures, and rather than filling it with their own team's thinking, they opened it up to other creators and marketers to write alongside them. Within three months it had over 7,000 views and a 61% open rate. The content is better for it, obviously - but the more interesting thing is what it signals about who the expertise belongs to. Not just Sprout, rather the community around them.

Different models, different scales, same principle underneath all of them: the community isn’t just the audience - it’s the collaborator.

And none of these are complicated ideas, they’re choices. Most brands simply don’t make them - either because they’re not thinking about it, or because there’s a manager somewhere who doesn’t trust their audience enough to let them in that last one is its own whole conversation, and something we’re addressing on stage at Scroll Forward this year.)

It’s more than delegation.

When most people hear “build with your community,” they picture one of two things:

A brand asking their audience to submit content so they don’t have to make it themselves

Or a creator who’s too stretched to do everything alone and needs people to plug the gaps.

Both of those things exist, and neither of them is what I’m talking about.

Because if “with” is just a nicer word for delegation, it’s still fundamentally a “for” mindset - you still see your community as a resource to draw from rather than a group of people who make the thing better by being part of it. The difference sounds subtle but it isn’t. Delegation says: I have a gap, you can fill it. “With” says: you bring something I don’t have, and this can’t be what it needs to be without you.

And if you’re reading this thinking that sounds like a nice idea but a bit vague - here’s what it actually looks like in practice.

The Insiders Club is our paid membership, 600-plus marketers across in-house, freelance, and agency. This year we built a community council: six members who each run something inside the club. Valeria hosts our monthly co-work sessions, Nefise keeps members across every note and resource from every session and webinar, Amy, Sonia, and Dayana each lead a community group (one for B2B marketers, one for freelancers, and one for personal brand builders) and Annie-Mai runs our quarterly trend talk calls.

I didn’t bring them in because I was stretched. I brought them in because Valeria creates an energy in a co-work session that I don’t, because Amy, Sonia, and Dayana are closer to those specific communities than I’ll ever be, because Annie-Mai lives and breathes trends in a way that makes our members better at their jobs. The club is something richer than I ever could have made alone - and that’s the point.

Case closed.

The best communities aren’t built by one brilliant person delivering endlessly to many, they’re built by many people, each bringing something the others couldn’t.

You’re not offloading when you invite people in - you’re unlocking.

Not for people, with them.
