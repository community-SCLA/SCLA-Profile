Summary of the SCLA Leadership Program:

The Society for Collegiate Leadership & Achievement (SCLA) is dedicated to honoring student achievement and empowering the next generation of leaders. The SCLA Leadership Program is built around three core objectives—Connections, Credentials, and Career—which guide students through a structured journey of leadership development, professional growth, and community building. Below is an overview of the program’s five key steps, designed to meet these objectives.

There are two distinct paths to these 5 steps:  Live Online (Live in exception) and Asynchronous.  Only Step 1 and Step 5 are available Live Online (Live in exception), while all five steps are available in asynchronous mode.  This means in a Live Online/Live format, steps 2-4 would still be asynchronous.

For both pathways, the 5 step journey includes:

Orientation/Welcome:

Objective: Introduce students to the SCLA, their chapter, and the overall leadership journey.

Includes: The True Colors Module, which helps students understand their leadership styles and communication preferences. This step focuses on building Connections within their chapter and learning about the program's framework.

SMART Goal Module:

Objective: Equip students to set and track their personal leadership goals.

Includes: An AI-powered tool that helps students define their leadership vision and measure their progress, contributing to their Credentials as emerging leaders.

Peer Connections:

Objective: Facilitate meaningful relationships with like-minded peers.

Includes: AI technology that matches students based on shared goals, academic interests, and personality traits. By fostering collaboration, this step emphasizes the development of Connections that support personal and professional growth.

Career Connections:

Objective: Provide students with opportunities to build their professional networks and career readiness.

Includes: Tools that connect students with professionals for short career chats, allowing them to gain insights and expand their network. This step focuses on helping students advance their Career through professional development.

Certification Completion:

Objective: Recognize and celebrate the student’s leadership journey and achievements.

Includes: A final self-reflection quiz and a personalized AI-generated summary video highlighting their growth. Upon completion of the program, students earn their Credentials and are invited to a certification celebration event (if applicable) or recognized individually.

Technical Implementation Instructions for AI Color Calculation:

Each word selection and scenario response should be assigned a predefined point value corresponding to its respective True Color.

The system will tally the total points for each color category (Blue, Gold, Green, Orange) based on the user's selections.

The highest-scoring color will be determined as the dominant color and displayed in the AI-generated video.

If two colors are tied, a secondary algorithm should evaluate responses from key personality indicators (e.g., word choice priority, scenario emphasis) to determine a final dominant color.

The AI-generated video should dynamically insert the student’s dominant color and its associated traits, along with tailored leadership insights.

To achieve your goals, you need a strategy and that is where the SMART Goal Framework comes in.  The SMART framework is so valuable for everything you need to plan and make taking action a breeze.

"SMART stands for:

Specific – What exactly do you want to accomplish?  A specific goal clearly defines what you want to accomplish. It answers questions like: What do I want to achieve? Why is this goal important? Who is involved?  Let’s use a personal example - we can start simple:  Let’s say you want to run the NYC or Boston Marathon next year.  It starts with a proclamation - a specific proclamation.

Specific: I’d like to start training every day to run a marathon.

Measurable – Next, how will you track your progress?  Measurable goals allow you to track your progress and determine when you've achieved your objective. This involves quantifying your goal with metrics or data points.

Measurable: for our example of the Marathon - you might say “ I will use a fitness tracking device to track my training progress as my mileage increases.

Achievable – Next, you have to ask yourself - Is this goal realistic given your time and resources?  An achievable goal is realistic and attainable within your current resources and constraints. It should challenge you but still be possible.

Achieveable:In our example,ask yourself questions and set a reasonable goal for yourself.   I’ve already run a half-marathon this year and have a solid baseline fitness level.

Relevant – Why does this goal matter to you?  A relevant goal aligns with your broader objectives and values. It ensures that the goal matters to you and is worthwhile.

Relevant: I value my health and wellness, and this fitness goal will help me sustain that.

Time-bound – When will you complete it?"*  Time-bound goals have a clear deadline or timeframe, creating a sense of urgency and prompting action.

Time-bound: The marathon is in June of next year, so I must be ready by then.

Once you’ve checked all these boxes, you’re left with a detailed goal-setting plan that keeps you focused and headed in the right direction. In our example, if you use the SMART Goal Framework, you can actually pace yourself and achieve the goal without burn out.

Using the SMART goal framework helps direct your actions toward reaching your goals. Framing your plans this way highlights potential challenges, letting you chart a detailed course that accounts for obstacles.

The Art of Asking:  How to Get the Most out of Networking

Whenever you’re messaging someone with an ask or an offer to connect, here are three rules of thumb to keep in mind:

1. Be concise.

a. Keep the subject line short, specific, and engaging.

b. Keep the reading time for the body of your message to under 30 seconds

2. Say what you mean.

a. Don’t dance around the ask or the offer, put it at the top of the message.

b. Don’t use passive language, take ownership of your intent.

3. Make it easy for them.

a. Consider their biggest hesitancy and what they would need to make a decision.

b. Reduce friction to get to the next step.

Think of networking as a mini-pitch and preparing yourself with at least 50% of the person researched (preparation) and your message composed in your mind (specificity). Same goes for asking a question—sketch out the opportunity for the other person, bring your own brainstormed ideas or solutions. Then, the other person can confirm, refute or expand on what you’ve proposed. It’s going to be a much more productive conversation from the jump, instead of something that starts and ends vaguely.

Appendix B

True Colors is a personality assessment model developed by Don Lowry in 1978. It is based on the theories of temperament types originally conceptualized by Dr. David Keirsey and the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI). True Colors simplifies complex personality theories into four color-coded categories that represent different personality styles, making it accessible and easy to use for a wide range of audiences.

The Four True Colors:

Blue: People who are empathetic, compassionate, and value relationships. They are driven by a need for harmony and emotional connections.

Gold: Individuals who are organized, responsible, and reliable. They tend to value structure, rules, and a sense of duty.

Green: Analytical, logical, and independent thinkers. Greens value knowledge, efficiency, and are driven by curiosity.

Orange: Energetic, spontaneous, and action-oriented. They thrive in dynamic environments and value freedom and excitement.

How It Works:

Participants in the True Colors assessment choose from descriptions or statements that best reflect their personality. The results assign them to a primary color (their dominant trait) and a secondary color that supports or complements their personality. The framework encourages understanding the strengths and potential blind spots of each color.

Applications of True Colors:

Educational Settings: True Colors is often used in schools and universities for student self-awareness and team-building activities.

Corporate/Workplace: Many organizations use True Colors for team building, improving communication, and enhancing collaboration. It helps employees understand and respect different working styles.

Personal Growth: It’s also used in personal development contexts, as it encourages individuals to reflect on their behavior, communication preferences, and relationships.

Comparison to Other Personality Models:

1. DISC vs. True Colors:

DISC: Focuses on four personality dimensions—Dominance, Influence, Steadiness, and Conscientiousness. DISC is highly focused on behavior, especially in workplace or performance settings.

True Colors: More holistic and simplified, tying personality traits to emotions and values rather than behavior. It’s often seen as easier to understand and implement in broader settings, such as educational or community environments.

Depth: DISC is generally considered more in-depth and professional, with strong applications in organizational development and leadership training. True Colors is less complex, making it ideal for quick, accessible insights.

2. MBTI vs. True Colors:

MBTI: Based on 16 personality types, MBTI dives deeper into specific combinations of preferences (e.g., Introversion vs. Extroversion, Thinking vs. Feeling). It’s seen as more intricate and detailed in terms of personality analysis.

True Colors: Simplifies MBTI’s more complex 16 types into 4 colors, making it more user-friendly and approachable for group settings.

Usage: MBTI is often used in both professional and clinical settings due to its depth, while True Colors is more frequently applied in educational or general team-building contexts.

3. StrengthsFinder vs. True Colors:

StrengthsFinder: Identifies a person’s top five strengths across 34 categories. It’s focused more on what people do well rather than their core personality.

True Colors: Focuses on personality and communication styles, with more emphasis on emotional understanding and interpersonal dynamics.

Usage: StrengthsFinder is widely used in career development and leadership coaching, while True Colors is more often used in personal growth or educational environments.

Strengths of True Colors:

Simplicity: True Colors is easy to understand and implement in group settings. It doesn’t require deep knowledge of psychology, which makes it accessible to many different audiences.

Relational Focus: True Colors ties directly into emotional values and communication, making it highly useful for relationship-building and teamwork.

Adaptability: It works well in educational environments, corporate team-building, and community groups where a quick, visual personality model is needed.

Immediate Usability: The color system allows for fast identification of personality types, which can be helpful for new groups trying to understand each other.

Limitations of True Colors:

Lack of Depth: While its simplicity is a strength, it can also be a limitation for those seeking a more nuanced understanding of personality. It doesn’t offer as much detail or exploration of personality traits as tools like MBTI or DISC.

Less Widely Researched: Compared to tools like DISC or MBTI, True Colors has less empirical research supporting its effectiveness. It is more of a practical tool than a rigorously researched psychological assessment.

Overgeneralization: With only four categories, it may oversimplify the complexity of human personality. Participants may not always fit neatly into one color, leading to a less personalized assessment.

How Much It's Used:

True Colors is widely used in educational, community, and corporate team-building settings. While not as popular in clinical or high-level organizational leadership training as tools like DISC or MBTI, it is valued for its simplicity and effectiveness in fostering quick understanding among participants.

True Colors vs. DISC and Other Tools:

Complexity: True Colors is more accessible but less detailed than DISC and MBTI, making it suitable for short workshops or introductory sessions where quick insights are needed.

Application: True Colors is typically used in more informal or educational settings, while DISC and MBTI may be used in more professional, coaching, or leadership development environments.

Emphasis: True Colors emphasizes emotional and relational communication, whereas DISC is more behavior-focused and MBTI is more comprehensive in mapping personality.

Conclusion:

True Colors offers a straightforward, engaging way to understand personality types, with a focus on communication and interpersonal relationships. It’s a great tool for environments where simplicity, accessibility, and immediate implementation are necessary. However, for those looking for a more nuanced understanding of personality or deeper organizational applications, tools like DISC or MBTI may offer greater insights.
