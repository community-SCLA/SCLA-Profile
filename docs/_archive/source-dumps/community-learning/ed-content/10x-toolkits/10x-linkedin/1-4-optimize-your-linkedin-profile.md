# 1.4 Optimize your LinkedIn profile

1.4 Optimizing your LinkedIn profile

Purpose of this exercise

LinkedIn is the default professional networking website that’s most commonly used across industries. Having a compelling LinkedIn profile will help you make new connections, maintain your network, and find career opportunities. Most employers will at least glance at your LinkedIn profile at some point during their hiring process, so it’s important to make a great impression and convey your value.

This worksheet will show you how to craft the most important sections of your profile.

Instructions

Open your LinkedIn profile (don’t have one yet?) and follow the guidelines and examples below to revamp your profile. Prioritize revamping the 2) Headline and 3) About sections of your profile first. You’ll review these two sections during your Team meeting this week. We’ll show you how to optimize all of your profile’s primary sections, but it may take you longer than the 60 minutes allotted for this module. If you have a faster timeline for your job search, we recommend spending more time completing your LinkedIn profile this week so you can start using it sooner!

1) Profile Photo

Your profile photo is the first part of your LinkedIn profile that a viewer will notice. First impressions are essential, and having a good profile photo will immediately make you more memorable and compelling.

A good profile photo:

Is up-to-date, high-resolution, and work-appropriate

Shows your face clearly and straight on (you should take up 60-80% of the photo)

Has a simple background that doesn’t include other people

If you don’t have a good professional photo of yourself, it’s worth taking your own professional headshots or even hiring a headshot photographer.

2) Headline

Your Headline is the most important section of your LinkedIn profile. Your Headline is the first thing to show when you appear in someone’s search results, on their newsfeed, in the “People you may know” section, and on LinkedIn job applications. When someone visits your profile, they’ll read your Headline before going any further down the page.

LinkedIn gives you up to 120 characters for your Headline. Their search algorithm prioritizes your headline keywords to determine if you show up when someone (like a recruiter or hiring manager) searches. An effective Headline is compelling, concise, and gives the viewer a reason to want to click on your name and continue reading about you.

You want your Headline to include the most tantalizing keywords that a recruiter or hiring manager is looking for. The reader should think “this is exactly what I’m looking for, I better read further.” Refer to your 0.6 Ideal Job Description for the top keywords to include here in your LinkedIn profile.

A compelling Headline should contain the following:

Your current (or most recent) role and organization (or industry).

If your official title is vague or misleading, describe your role using the language you think most people would understand. If a recruiter is likely to recognize the name of the organization you currently work for, include it.

Keywords for your desired role/industry

Include a handful of words that a recruiter would likely search for to find candidates, especially if you’re pursuing a role that’s different from your current one.

Keywords for any skills, experience, or credentials relevant to desired role/industry

Choose the most needed skills/experience/credentials that a recruiter is likely to be looking for in a top candidate.

*If you’re currently unemployed, start with your most recent or most relevant title. Do not say that you’re “open to opportunities” or something similar in your Headline (do this instead). Use this to showcase your value, not telegraph that you’re unemployed. If you want to do something very different from any past experience, give yourself the title you are looking for, not an unrelated old title.

3) About Section

The About section is your chance to stand out from the crowd and showcase your uniqueness. You can think of your About section as a personal pitch where you’ll highlight the type of value you create, the skills you bring to the table, and the impact your work has.

Your About section should:

Be 4-6 sentences in length

Include a catchy introduction that draws readers in

Be written in the first person

Have a skimmable format (short sentences, bullets, line breaks, etc.)

Generic About section formula:

Start with an attention-grabbing sentence

State your unique value proposition, so it’s clear what you bring to the table

Mention the relevant skills, experience, and credentials that back up your value proposition

Share an example(s) of how you create impact

End by inviting the reader to connect with you

Use several job-specific keywords (this helps with your search ranking) and make it really clear why you’re a great candidate for this specific position. Think about what a hiring manager would be most excited to see in a candidate, and provide evidence that you have what they’re looking for. Make sure it’s really clear what value you could deliver to the organization that hires you.

Julie (data scientist who wants to do a similar role in a social impact field):

I love helping organizations deliver real social impact by understanding data.

As a Data Scientist, I find and tell stories through data that help organizations make smarter decisions. I use my machine learning skills and knowledge of programming languages (C++, Python, and R) to create complex statistical models that predict user behavior and inform product/program strategy.

At Klik Marketing, I design statistical models that help our clients achieve an average of 50% increase in revenue. Previously, my work has uncovered customer insights that have turned into new products and features that improve retention and LTV.

If you want a data-informed approach to address a social challenge, let’s talk!

David (classroom teacher who wants to move into administration):

Children are our future, and they deserve a shot at living extraordinary lives.

As a K-12 educator, I help my students believe in themselves and achieve great things while also redesigning the education structures I work within.

I use my coaching and process improvement skills to help my colleagues develop as professionals. I’ve implemented new softwares, managed budgets, and assisted district administrators across many initiatives.

In 2019, I led the roll out of a $35k PD initiative for 32 teachers with on-time delivery and a 92% satisfaction rate. This year I led a committee to select and roll out a new student management system, which was launched ahead of time and under budget.

Connect with me if you’re interested in improving public education administration or teacher professional development.

Maria (Project manager trying to land a new job in a similar role at a different company):

I love leading teams to launch new FinTech products.

As a Project Manager at BOA, I lead a cross-functional team of 9 to develop new consumer financial products and eCommerce tools for businesses.

I use my project management skills to create timelines, manage budgets, and optimize processes. In 2019 I led the development of a new product that netted over $4M in revenue and served over 300,000 customers. Last year, my team saved the company $3.2M in lost revenue by redesigning a customer renewal process.

I’m always looking for bigger opportunities to create new products and serve FinTech customers better. Reach out if you’d like to collaborate!

4) Experience Section

This section is just like the experience section of your resume; it’s an opportunity to outline your professional history. When a recruiter or hiring manager sees your Experience section, they’ll be looking for examples of times that you’ve made a real impact for your former employers. They care about your impact, not just your responsibilities. And they certainly don’t want to read through a bunch of text that’s not relevant to the job they’re evaluating you for.

Each of your work experiences should follow this format:

Title: Use your official title, or slightly modify your title so the reader is more likely to understand it.

Description: Use this section to explain your role and the value you created

Summary line: Provide a one-sentence summary of your role so the reader can quickly understand your primary responsibilities and skill sets

Impact Evidence: Share any significant achievements you had in this role. Try to quantify the impact your work had, if possible. Use your work from 1.5 Skills, Skills Statements, and Impact Evidence.

Follow these two steps to optimize the Experience section of your profile:

Step 1: Reinforce relevant information. Go through the Impact Evidence you have for each of your prior experiences. Each bullet point should be relevant to the position you’re pursuing, and showcase the key skills/experience/credentials that are most likely to be mentioned in the Job Description.

Step 2: Remove irrelevant information. Remove any bullets that are not clearly related to the position you’re pursuing (these are just noise; fewer words on the page is better because it highlights the important information).

5) Education and Certifications Sections

This section allows you to show off your educational background to demonstrate that you have specific competencies/expertise, and that you have the capacity to learn. Employers generally care less about this section if you have more years of work experience. Work experience will almost always count for more than formal educational background.

Your Education section should:

Include your highest level of education (If you have a Bachelor’s degree or higher, you don’t need to include any information about your high school education)

Highlight the most important groups, organizations, publications, or relevant activities you were a part of that demonstrate leadership abilities and communicate your values or interests. You can include this information in the “Activities and Societies” and “Description” boxes within the education section. (For example: Served as President of Alpha Kappa Psi Business Fraternity or Founded the AI and Future of Work Club)

Your Certifications section should:

List any relevant and up-to-date certifications or licenses, dates completed, and Credential IDs (if applicable)
