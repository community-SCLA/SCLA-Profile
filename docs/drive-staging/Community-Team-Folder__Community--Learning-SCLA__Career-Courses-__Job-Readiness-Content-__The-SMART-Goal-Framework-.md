**Slide 4**

S pecific  -  What exactly do you want to accomplish?
A specific goal clearly defines what you want to accomplish. It answers questions like:
What do I want to achieve?
Why is this goal important?
Who is involved?
Let’s use a personal example - we can start simple:  Let’s say you want to run the NYC or Boston Marathon next year.  It starts with a proclamation - a specific proclamation.
Specific: I’d like to start training every day to run a marathon.

---

**Slide 5**

Measurable  – how will you track your progress?
Measurable goals allow you to track your progress and determine when you've achieved your objective. This involves quantifying your goal with metrics or data points.
Measurable: for our example of the Marathon - you might say “ I will use a fitness tracking device to track my training progress as my mileage increases.

---

**Slide 6**

Achievable  – Is this goal realistic given your time and resources?
An achievable goal is realistic and attainable within your current resources and constraints. It  should challenge you but still be possible .
Achievable:In our example, sk yourself questions and set a reasonable goal for yourself.   I’ve already run a half-marathon this year and have a solid baseline fitness level.

---

**Slide 7**

Relevant  – Why does this goal matter to you?
A relevant goal aligns with your broader  objectives and values . It ensures that the goal matters to you and is worthwhile.
Relevant: I value my health and wellness, and this fitness goal will help me sustain that.

---

**Slide 8**

Time-bound  – When will you complete it?
Time-bound goals have a clear deadline or timeframe, creating a sense of urgency and prompting action.
Time-bound: The marathon is in June of next year, so I must be ready by then.

Using the SMART framework helps you break down larger career ambitions into actionable steps.
This clarity increases your motivation, helps you monitor your progress, and improves your chances of success in a competitive job market.
