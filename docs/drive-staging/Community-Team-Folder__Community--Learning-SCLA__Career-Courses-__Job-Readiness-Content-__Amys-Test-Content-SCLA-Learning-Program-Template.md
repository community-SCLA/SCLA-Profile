Part 1: Identifying your Technical Skills vs. Durable Skills

[Dynamic Video]

Technical skills, often referred to as “hard skills”, are usually studied or developed through rigorous training and practice. They are usually common in certain types of jobs, and often involve using special tools, software, or equipment.

Durable skills, often referred to as “soft skills,” are much more generic, and can be used across a variety of roles and industries. These skills have more to do with your attitude and behavior and usually don’t require a specific software or tool to use.

Assessment

List out your Technical and Durable skills. Identify at least 10 skills total. If you need help identifying what your skills are, check out the resources we’ve included.

[Video and workbook]

It’s important to recognize that all of your Durable skills are also transferable to other roles and industries. And don’t discount the value of these “soft” skills - they are often more critical for success than some of the Technical Skills that you can always learn through a course or training program!

Part 2: Turn your skills into Skills Statements

In this section, you're going to transform your skills from above (which can often feel broad or vague) into “Skills Statements," which are short statements that clearly communicate what your skills are and how specifically you use them.

Skills Statements are great talking points when networking or interviewing, and can also be used in cover letters.

Skills Statements follow this format: I use my _______ skills to _____________________

Here are some examples:

●       I use my communication skills to turn complex information into easy-to-understand instructions

●       I use my project management skills to design efficient project plans and then facilitate their execution on time and on budget

●       I use my leadership skills to orient my team around a goal and remove their barriers to get there

●       I use my marketing skills to reach a target audience and compel them to take action

An effective Skills Statement is easy to understand and NOT specific to one particular job (don’t say “I use my leadership skills to make sure the AWS team files the TPS reports on time”).

Part 3: Back up your Skills Statements with Impact Evidence

Your Skills Statements tell someone what you do, but they don’t provide evidence that you do it well.

This is where most people go wrong when talking about their professional experience (especially in resumes). Most people talk about what they did, but they don’t provide compelling evidence to show the impact they achieved, which is really what employers care about.

In this section, you will craft Impact Evidence and concise statements explaining how you’ve used your skills to achieve valuable impact. You will use this Impact Evidence framework when writing bullet points for your resume or responding to questions during an interview.

Impact Evidence follows this format:

[Action verb] + [context for what you did] + [impact your contribution had].

Here are some examples:

●       Skills Statement: I use my communication skills to turn complex information into easy-to-understand instructions

○       Impact Evidence: Developed virtual training program for 2,500 employees, reducing training time by 30% and saving over $100k in training costs.

●       Skills Statement: I use my project management skills to design efficient project plans then facilitate their execution on time and on budget

○       Impact Evidence: Designed and managed the project plan for a $2.2M IT infrastructure project that finished on time and 10% under budget.

●       Skills Statement: I use my leadership skills to orient my team around a goal and remove their barriers to get there

○       Impact Evidence: Led a team of 13 to exceed departmental goals for 2019, increasing revenue by 15% and improving client retention by 5%.

●       Skills Statement: I use my marketing skills to reach a target audience and compel them to take action

○       Impact Evidence: Launched a new marketing campaign that increased customer conversions by 3x and generated over $85k in new revenue.

The key to effective Impact Evidence is having a quantifiable outcome you can share. It’s ok to make rough estimates when trying to quantify the impact of your prior experience as long as you can explain how you arrived at your numbers.

Here are some examples of ways you can quantify your impact:

●       Time saved - “Reduced the time by 20%”, “Saved 10 hours per week

●       Revenue generated - “Increased revenue by 7%,” “Generated $230k additional revenue”

●       Progress towards goals - “Surpassed goal by 20%,” “Improved retention by 8%”

●       People served - “Reached over 35,000 customers,” “Managed a team of 25”

●       Cost savings - “Reduced costs by 37%,” “Saved over $1.2M annually”

●       Recognition received - “Received two awards,” “Cited by 24 experts”

But what if you can’t quantify what you did?

If you can’t figure out a way to quantify some aspect of your work, the next best thing is to highlight why it mattered. Don’t just say what you did, talk about why it was valuable. Here are some examples:

●       Answered phones, took messages: Relayed important communications in a timely fashion to executives to ensure smooth operation of the program.

●       Counseled patients: Provided professional counseling to at-risk patients, resulting in lowered stress and improved compliance.

●       Taught 4th graders: Developed and led instruction for a class of 28 fourth graders, surpassing educational goals for the year.

Appendix: How to Identify Your Skills

Below are three strategies to help you identify your professional skills:

List out your responsibilities (for your current or previous roles or volunteer work), then think about what skills you needed to use to accomplish those responsibilities.

Think about your greatest professional achievements, then respond to the following questions. Once you’ve written down your responses, read through them and try to tease out what skills you must have used.

What did you do and how did you do it?

How did you overcome any challenges that arose during the process?

What personal qualities played a part in you reaching these achievements?

If these accomplishments involved a team, how did you interact with others to achieve the goals?
