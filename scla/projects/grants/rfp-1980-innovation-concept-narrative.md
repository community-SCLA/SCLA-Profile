## Company Background and Experience

The Society for Collegiate Leadership & Achievement (SCLA) is a national college-to-career organization founded in 2015. Over eleven years, SCLA has grown to serve more than 100,000 members across 850+ partner campuses — universities, community colleges, and online institutions. Our membership is open to any learner, regardless of whether their institution has an on-campus SCLA chapter. Students without a chapter can join through online membership, receiving the same certifications, career tools, and credential platform access as on-campus members.

SCLA does not merely advise students about credentials — we build, issue, and deliver them. Our flagship Career Readiness Certification is an accredited, skills-assessed program tied to the ISPI strengths assessment framework, supported by 550+ skill development modules, and recognized by a national employer network. More than 5,600 learners have earned the certification, which carries a 4.7 learner satisfaction rating. Completing the certification creates a pathway to college credit — a recognition mechanism that matters most to non-traditional and returning adult learners.

SCLA operates a live digital credential wallet at app.thescla.org/member/credentials. Members use this wallet today to store, manage, and share verified records of academic achievements, certifications, skill badges, and career milestones. The wallet supports learner-controlled sharing, granular privacy controls, and direct credential presentation to employers and institutions. This is not a prototype or a concept — it is production infrastructure serving active learners across the country.

## Question 1: Vision of Equity
*Suggested length: 3 pages | 20 points*

### The problem is not a skills gap. It is a recognition gap.

Millions of Californians have developed real, demonstrable skills — through work, community, military service, caregiving, lived experience, and non-traditional education. What they lack is not capability. It is a system that can see what they have built.

The bachelor's degree remains the dominant proxy for employability — not because it is the most accurate signal of competence, but because it is the most legible signal to hiring systems designed around it. A person who spent four years completing community college coursework, a career certification, and two workforce training programs has demonstrated more applied professional capability than many degree holders. None of that shows up in an automated screening filter.

SCLA's work begins at exactly this gap. We serve learners who earn credentials outside of traditional transcript pipelines: through certifications, assessments, skill badges, peer-recognized achievements, and experiential learning programs. Our credential wallet was built for a learner whose most meaningful professional asset does not appear on a college transcript. That is our baseline design assumption — not an edge case we are retrofitting.

### Structural barriers the Career Passport must address

**Learners without bachelor's degrees**
Traditional credential systems assume a degree at the center. SCLA's platform is built degree-optional. A learner who completed two years of community college, earned a Career Readiness Certification, and holds verified skill badges has a portfolio of real achievement. Today, none of those assets speak to each other in a way employer systems understand. The Career Passport changes that — if built on open standards that make every verified credential legible to every conformant hiring system.

**Low-income learners**
Credential verification is often expensive, slow, and inaccessible. Learners who need to share proof of achievement for a job application, a benefits program, or a licensing process should not need to navigate multiple registrar offices, pay transcript fees, or wait two weeks for a document that may arrive in the wrong format. SCLA's wallet puts all credentials in one place, with one-click sharing directly from a learner's phone.

**Learners from immigrant and undocumented communities**
These learners are disproportionately present in California's workforce and disproportionately excluded from credential systems tied to formal institutional identity verification. A skills-forward wallet that represents what a person can do — independent of immigration-linked institutional records — expands the surface area of economic opportunity for people who have historically had to prove themselves twice to get half as far.

**Military service members and veterans**
Military training produces significant, transferable skills that civilian hiring systems routinely fail to interpret. A structured digital credential wallet can translate military occupational specialties and training records into employer-readable format without requiring a veteran to re-explain their service in every application. SCLA's Career Readiness programming, accessible through online membership, serves this population today.

**Formerly incarcerated individuals**
Re-entry success is strongly correlated with employment. Formerly incarcerated Californians often cannot produce traditional credentials — their work history is invisible, their institutional relationships severed. A portable, learner-controlled credential wallet creates a new starting point: here is what I have learned and what I can do. It is a record that belongs to the person, not the institution.

### A specific scenario

Maria is 36. She attended community college in the San Joaquin Valley for two years but left to care for an ill parent. She has since worked in healthcare administration, completed an online digital skills certification, and earned SCLA's Career Readiness Certification through online membership — available to her because her school no longer had an active SCLA chapter.

She has real skills. She has completed credentials. She does not have a degree.

When Maria applies for a program coordinator position at a regional health network, she is eliminated in the first round of automated screening because the ATS filters require a bachelor's degree. Her digital skills certification lives in a PDF on her desktop. Her SCLA credential is not in any system the ATS can read. She is invisible to the hiring process before a human ever sees her name.

With the California Career Passport, Maria's wallet contains verified, machine-readable records of her skills certification, her SCLA career credential, and her healthcare work experience — issued by recognized entities, formatted to the W3C Verifiable Credentials standard, readable by any conformant employer system. She is not screened out. She gets the interview.

### SCLA's equity design principles for the Career Passport

**Learner-controlled from day one.**
The wallet is the learner's property, not the institution's. SCLA does not control what a learner shares, with whom, or when. Sharing is always a holder-initiated action.

**No prerequisite institutional affiliation.**
A learner does not need to be currently enrolled, in good standing, or affiliated with a participating campus to hold credentials in the wallet. A learner between jobs, between programs, or between institutions does not lose access to their record.

**Accessible on low-end and shared devices.**
The wallet is built mobile-first and tested on entry-level Android and iOS devices. Credential viewing will function offline. We do not design for the learner who has a MacBook — we design for the learner sharing one phone with a family member.

**Plain language throughout.**
Every credential, every sharing prompt, and every privacy control is written for a reader who did not go to college. Language is tested with real users from target populations — not assumed by the team that built the platform.

**Language access as infrastructure, not afterthought.**
Spanish-language support is included in the pilot interface. A documented roadmap for additional language access — tied to actual pilot population data — will be delivered with the Phase 3 proposal.

### What California is doing — and why SCLA is ready for it

California is leading a national shift from degree-based to skills-informed hiring. Executive orders, employer coalitions, and community college policy changes are all pointing the same direction. SCLA has operated at the leading edge of this shift for eleven years. We credential skills. We recognize non-traditional achievement. We serve learners that traditional academic systems do not reach.

The Career Passport is the infrastructure layer this movement has been waiting for. SCLA is the right organization to build it because we have already spent a decade building the learner relationships, the credential ecosystem, and the technical platform that make it real.

### What success looks like

At pilot end: 50 California learners — drawn from populations that current systems underserve — have a portable, verified credential wallet with at least three credential types from at least two issuers. They can share their credentials with an employer or institution via a single link, without phone calls, registrar requests, or explanations. They know what their credentials say and what they mean.

At statewide scale: any Californian — regardless of degree status, immigration status, incarceration history, military background, or income — can hold and present verified proof of what they know and what they can do. The Career Passport becomes the infrastructure layer beneath every jobs program, every workforce grant, and every hiring decision in the state.

## Question 2: Technical Architecture and Open Standards
*Suggested length: 3 pages | 25 points*

### Architecture philosophy: the holder is the source of truth

SCLA's proposed architecture for the California Career Passport is a **hybrid custodial model** built around one non-negotiable principle: a holder's credential portfolio must survive any single point of failure, including SCLA itself.

### Custodial vs. non-custodial vs. hybrid

A **purely non-custodial** architecture places full cryptographic key management on the holder. This is appropriate for sophisticated users and unusable for most populations this RFP is designed to serve. A formerly incarcerated re-entrant or a low-income community college student cannot be expected to manage their own private keys. Designing for non-custodial-only is designing for exclusion.

A **purely custodial** architecture makes the vendor the single point of failure. If SCLA loses this contract or ceases operation, credentials on a custodial platform without export capability become inaccessible to the people who earned them.

SCLA proposes a **hybrid approach:**
- SCLA manages cryptographic key infrastructure on behalf of holders (custodial by default — no technical knowledge required)
- Holders can export their cryptographic keys and full credential archive at any time, transferring to any conformant non-custodial wallet
- All credentials are issued in open, portable formats — even if SCLA's platform ceases operation tomorrow, credentials stored by the holder remain valid and verifiable by any conformant verifier

### Open standards compliance

SCLA's Career Passport implementation will comply with the following open standards:

| Standard | Role |
|---|---|
| W3C Verifiable Credentials Data Model (VCDM) 2.0 | Core credential format for all issued credentials |
| Open Badges v3.0 (OBv3) | Badge-type credential issuance and display |
| OID4VP (OpenID for Verifiable Presentations) | Credential presentation to verifiers |
| VC-API | Issuer and verifier API integration |
| DID (Decentralized Identifiers) | Credential subject and issuer identification |
| SD-JWT (Selective Disclosure JWT) | Selective disclosure implementation |

### What happens if SCLA loses the contract

This question is not hypothetical — it is the most important governance question in this RFP, and every proposer should answer it clearly.

If SCLA loses this contract, is acquired, or ceases to operate:

1. All credential data is immediately exportable by every holder in standard W3C VCDM 2.0 format. This is available before, during, and after any contract transition.
2. Issued credentials remain cryptographically valid and verifiable by any conformant verifier. SCLA's servers are not required for verification — the cryptographic proof is in the credential, not in our infrastructure.
3. The complete credential dataset is transferred to the District in open format within 30 days of contract termination.
4. SCLA will open-source the pilot implementation under a permissive license, so the State can continue operating it or transition it to another vendor without starting from scratch.

### Privacy architecture

**Selective disclosure**
Holders choose which credential claims to share in each presentation. A learner presenting to an employer can share their skill certifications without revealing their GPA, home address, or other credentials in their wallet. Selective disclosure is implemented through SD-JWT, with a user interface that makes the selection process comprehensible to users with no technical background. This is tested with real users before deployment.

**Unlinkability**
Each credential presentation generates a fresh, unlinkable proof. The same credential presented to Employer A and Employer B produces presentations that cannot be correlated without the holder's explicit participation. Issuers cannot track when or to whom their credentials are presented.

**No metadata leakage to issuers**
SCLA's issuance infrastructure does not receive notification when a holder presents a credential to a verifier. The issuer's role ends at issuance. An employer verification does not create a feedback loop to the issuing institution.

**Logging and holder control**
SCLA logs: credential issuance events (issuer, credential type, timestamp), holder-initiated sharing events (holder ID, timestamp — not recipient identity), and system errors. No credential content is logged server-side after issuance. Holders can view their complete personal audit trail in their wallet dashboard at any time.

### Credential display as standards evolve

SCLA's platform uses a display layer that is architecturally decoupled from the underlying cryptographic structure. When a new credential type or metadata schema emerges, display templates are updated without touching the credential itself. When W3C VCDM 3.0 or Open Badges v4 are published, SCLA will update the display layer and, where required, offer holders a migration path — with plain-language explanation of what is changing and why.

## Question 3: A Wallet for All Credentials
*Suggested length: 3 pages | 15 points*

### SCLA has been managing credential diversity for eleven years. This is not a new problem.

Our platform receives accredited certifications, employer-issued skill badges, academic credit records from partner institutions, and experiential learning records. Each comes with different metadata richness. Each requires different display logic. Each holder expects their credential to be readable — regardless of where it came from or how technically sophisticated the issuer was.

### Handling W3C VCDM credentials with varying metadata richness

SCLA's wallet applies a three-tier display strategy:

**Rich credentials** — full metadata: issuer logo, learner name, competency map, assessment scores, expiration date, evidence links, and endorsements — receive a full display with all available context rendered in plain language.

**Standard credentials** — core VCDM fields: issuer, subject, issuance date, credential type — receive a clean, readable card with clearly labeled fields and an "issued by" trust indicator.

**Sparse credentials** — minimal metadata, unrecognized schema — receive a graceful fallback: holder name, issuing entity name, date, credential type label (if parseable), and an honest disclosure: "This credential's details could not be fully displayed. The credential itself is valid and verifiable." The holder is never shown a broken UI. The verifier receives the same honest disclosure.

### Accommodating issuers of all sizes

**Large issuers** — community college districts, state workforce agencies, employer training programs — connect via VC-API with automated issuance pipelines, organizational branding, and status list endpoints for revocation management.

**Small issuers** — a 10-week coding bootcamp, a community re-entry program, a workforce nonprofit — issue through SCLA's Issuer Portal: a guided, web-based interface requiring no API integration, no developer, and no procurement process. An organization with one laptop and a list of graduates can issue a verifiable credential in under 10 minutes.

This matters for California's equity populations. The organizations serving formerly incarcerated individuals, immigrant workers, and military veterans are often small, under-resourced, and technically limited. If the Career Passport can only be issued by large institutions with API teams, it will systematically exclude the credentials that matter most to the people this RFP is designed to serve.

### Graceful degradation for unknown issuers

The wallet does not reject credentials from unrecognized issuers. It displays them with a transparent disclosure: "We could not verify this issuer's registration with the California Career Passport network. This does not mean the credential is invalid — it means the issuing organization has not yet been registered. Contact [issuer name] or the State Career Passport office if you have questions."

Holders can still share these credentials. Verifiers see the same disclosure. Over time, the State builds out the trusted issuer registry; the disclosure disappears as issuers are added. No credential is silently buried or hidden.

### Multi-issuer interoperability experience

SCLA currently manages wallets containing credentials from multiple simultaneous issuers for every active member. Our partnership transition from NIC/CGHS required receiving academic credit records originally issued by a third-party organization and integrating them alongside SCLA-issued credentials in the same wallet — a multi-issuer operation in production. [CONFIRM: Any additional multi-issuer integration examples from current platform to cite here.]

For the pilot, SCLA proposes a minimum of three credential types from at least two issuers: one SCLA-issued career credential, one academic or workforce credential from a participating California institution, and one open badge-type credential from a workforce partner. This tests the full range of issuer sizes and credential types in a live environment.

### Credential lifecycle management

**Revoked credentials**
Revocation is propagated via the issuer's status list endpoint (Bitstring Status List v1.0). The wallet checks status on every presentation, not just on receipt. A revoked credential is flagged with a plain-language explanation: "This credential was revoked by [Issuer] on [Date]. Contact [Issuer] if you believe this is an error." The credential remains in the wallet — the holder owns it — but its status is accurately disclosed.

**Superseded credentials**
When a renewed certification replaces a previous one, both versions are retained with clear versioning labels. Holders choose which version to share. This matters for learners who want to demonstrate a history of ongoing professional development, not just a single point-in-time credential.

**Issuer infrastructure changes**
SCLA monitors issuer endpoint health. If an issuer's verification endpoint goes offline, affected credentials are flagged "temporarily unverifiable" — they remain in the wallet and can still be shared, with transparent disclosure to verifiers. SCLA notifies the issuer directly and follows up within 5 business days.

### Portability

A holder can export their complete credential portfolio in a single W3C VCDM 2.0-compliant archive at any time, from any screen in the wallet. This export is readable by any conformant wallet. The holder is never locked into SCLA's platform. Portability is not a setting — it is always on.

## Question 4: Integration with California's Education and Workforce Ecosystem
*Suggested length: 3 pages | 10 points*

### A credential that only works in one system doesn't work.

SCLA's integration strategy starts from the verifier's perspective: what does an employer hiring manager actually do with a credential presentation? What does a community college admissions office need? What does a workforce agency require? The Career Passport must be useful to the systems these learners are trying to enter — not just legible to the learners themselves.

### Employer integration

**Large employers and enterprise ATS systems** (Workday, iCIMS, Greenhouse, Lever, Taleo) are moving toward structured skills data via HROPEN and similar standards. SCLA will provide a standard verification link and a JSON-LD credential export in formats compatible with emerging ATS skills integrations. For employers with existing API capability, SCLA will provide a conformant OID4VP verification endpoint.

**Small and medium employers** — the majority of California's hiring market — do not have sophisticated HR technology. For them, the Career Passport must work in a Gmail inbox. A learner sends a PDF-formatted credential summary with a QR code linking to live verification. No employer portal account. No API integration. No new software to install. Scan the code; get a verified result in plain language in under three seconds.

**Employer verification portal**
SCLA maintains a free, public, account-free verification portal. Paste a credential link or scan a QR code — the result is returned in plain language: "This credential is valid. Issued by [Organization] on [Date]. Credential type: [Type]. Holder: [Name as specified by holder]." No registration required to verify.

We will support both automated VC-API issuance pipelines for technically equipped districts and manual issuer portal workflows for smaller institutions.

### Government HR and workforce agency integration

California's Employment Development Department (EDD), CalHR, and regional workforce development boards operate with varying technical sophistication. SCLA will participate in the State's issuer and verifier working group to ensure credential presentations are compatible with current State HR systems. For the pilot, all presentations include a human-readable PDF summary alongside machine-readable verification — no State agency needs to update its systems to begin accepting Career Passport credentials during the pilot period.

### Relationship to California's digital identity infrastructure

SCLA proposes integration with the CA DMV mobile driver's license (mDL) as an optional identity verification pathway for wallet account creation. Using an already-trusted state credential for identity bootstrapping reduces friction for learners and increases verifier confidence without creating a new identity silo. This is optional — learners who do not have a CA mDL can verify identity through alternative pathways that do not exclude undocumented or low-income populations. ### Ecosystem philosophy

SCLA's goal is not to become the platform California's credential ecosystem depends on. It is to be the infrastructure layer that makes any credential legible to any system. The Career Passport succeeds when learners can take their credentials anywhere — including completely outside of SCLA's ecosystem. We design for portability and openness because lock-in is the failure mode this RFP is explicitly designed to prevent.

## Question 5: Governance, Trust, and Long-Term Stewardship
*Suggested length: 3 pages | 10 points*

### The populations this program serves have been failed by systems before. Governance must earn trust — not assume it.

A learner who has navigated re-entry, workforce displacement, or a system that treated their education as invisible approaches a new platform with justified skepticism. The governance model for the Career Passport must be designed to earn and maintain trust at every level: the individual learner, the issuing institution, the verifying employer, and the State of California.

### Credential portability and continuity

SCLA commits to the following unconditional guarantees, enforceable as contractual obligations:

1. All credentials in the Career Passport are available for export by the holder in open W3C VCDM 2.0 format at all times — before, during, and after any contract transition.
2. If SCLA loses this contract or ceases to operate, the State receives a complete, open-format credential dataset backup within 30 days.
3. All issued credentials remain cryptographically valid and independently verifiable for a minimum of 7 years from issuance, regardless of SCLA's continued involvement.
4. Full credential portability to any conformant wallet is available from the first day of pilot operation — not added in a later release.

### Open verifier ecosystem

SCLA commits to maintaining a public, open verification endpoint that any conformant verifier can use without prior agreement, registration fee, or proprietary tooling. A small employer in Fresno, a licensing board in Sacramento, and a community college in San Diego can all verify a Career Passport credential using the same open endpoint, with no relationship to SCLA required.

### Standards currency

SCLA will maintain active participation in:
- W3C Credentials Community Group
- IMS Global (Open Badges working group)
- California's Career Passport governance body, once established by the State

When a relevant standard is updated, SCLA will publish a plain-language impact summary for learners, issuers, and verifiers within 60 days of the standard's release and implement required platform updates within 180 days. No standard change will result in credential loss or inaccessibility for existing holders.

### Community governance

SCLA will establish a Career Passport Advisory Council with representation from:
- Enrolled pilot learners, including at least one representative from each equity population named in the RFP
- Partner California community college institutions
- Employer verifiers, including small employers
- California State workforce agency representatives
- Privacy, accessibility, and language access experts

Advisory Council members are compensated for their time. The Council reviews platform changes before deployment, flags equity concerns, and provides direct input on feature prioritization. Council recommendations are published publicly with SCLA's responses.

### State oversight

SCLA welcomes contractual State oversight, including:
- Quarterly reporting on credential issuance volume, learner demographics, wallet activity, and completion rates disaggregated by population
- Annual third-party privacy audit with results shared with the District and Chancellor's Office
- Incident disclosure: any data breach or credential integrity issue disclosed to the State within 24 hours, to affected holders within 72 hours, in plain language — not legal boilerplate

### Plain-language change communication

SCLA maintains a public, plain-language changelog. Every update affecting how learners store, share, or view credentials is described in plain language before it is deployed. Learners receive email notification of any change that affects their data or sharing behaviors. Changes to privacy settings or data access are communicated with at least 14 days' notice before taking effect.

SCLA commits to WCAG 2.1 AA compliance for all learner-facing interfaces for the duration of this contract. Spanish-language support is included in the pilot interface. Language access coverage will expand based on pilot population data and is a contractual obligation in the production proposal — not an aspiration that can be deferred.
