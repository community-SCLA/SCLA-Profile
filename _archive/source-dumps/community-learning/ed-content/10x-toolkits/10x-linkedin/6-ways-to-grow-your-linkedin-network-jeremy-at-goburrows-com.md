# 6 Ways to Grow Your LinkedIn Network - Jeremy at GoBurrows.com

6 Ways to Grow Your LinkedIn Network - from GoBurrows.com

1. Reach out to assistants in your city

One of the most effective ways I’ve grown my LI network is to reach out to assistants in my area, inviting them to gather for a happy hour, or lunch, etc. LI makes this very easy for you to do.

Using the standard LI search page, type in the title “Executive Assistant”

Add/select your city in the “Locations” filter

Add/select the filter for “2nd and 3rd connections”

Hit “apply.”

The results page will likely have thousands of assistants within miles of you.

Go to each profile, one by one, click “Connect”

Then click “Add a note” and say something like this, “Hi (First Name), I’m branching out to meet other assistants in (City name) and would love to meet for lunch or put together a local EA happy hour. Interested? -(Your name)”

Hit “Send”

2. Connect with assistants around the world

In today’s e-connected world, you can also have meaningful connections with assistants on the other side of the world. Whether you find a LI group of hundreds of assistants (Like this one), or you create your own group and invite assistants to it, there’s no reason an assistant has to be within driving distance to be able to connect with them.

Here’s an example of what you could say when reaching out to assistants outside of your city…

“Hi (First Name) - I’ve been an EA for several years, but failed to grow my network most of my career. I’m hoping to change that and would love to connect and learn from you. Would you be up for an informal meet up when I’m in town (or a phone call if you’re not going to be in their town)?”

2.1. Join active groups and contribute to them/help people.

Another quick tip: join and participate in LI groups that are active – like this one – and engage in posts, share articles, help those who ask for help, and connect with other assistants.

3. Engage with other posts by fellow assistants

Don’t just “Like” everything. Leave thoughtful comments on other posts you find interesting. Ask questions, and share with friends who would think the topic is interesting.

Don’t be a passive consumer, be an active user who contributes to the global assistant community.

4. Write engaging posts on LinkedIn

You should also write posts from your account that are not necessarily in a group, but just on your main LI profile as a standard post.

One type of post: Ask questions related to your network.

This LI post was one of my most popular LinkedIn posts with the most comments and likes and views.

Being an assistant can be dehumanizing at times.

I'm working on a few podcast episodes and blog posts sharing my personal experience with this topic, and I could use your help.

Assistants - have you experienced one or more of the following?

1. People ask how your boss is doing (but don't ask about you).

2. People befriend you to get closer to your executive.

3. So called "friends" are nowhere to be found when you switch companies or bosses.

Would you comment with the number(s) you can relate to most?

It’s a topic I am passionate about because I’ve experienced it before, but I simply asked other assistants to share if they could relate.

Another type of post would be to share articles or videos that are helpful.

Lastly, you can write longer posts as articles using LinkedIn’s article feature, which is basically your own blog feed within LinkedIn.

The point of each of these? Add value! Here’s a great example of a post that adds value.

5. Switch your “Connect” button to the “Follow” button

This may or may not work for you, but if you get traffic to your profile because you’re more engaged or you have another website sending people to your LinkedIn profile, it can work very well.

Essentially, this allows people the opportunity to “one-click” follow you when they’re viewing your profile, instead of having to hit “connect” then decide if they’re going to “add a note” or not, and so on.

To give you some context, as of September 2023, I have 23,600+ connections, but I have 37,600+ followers.

Followers still see and engage with my posts, I’m just not connected with them (meaning I don’t follow them).

6. Update your public settings (what people can see when they’re not connected with you)

There are some simple settings you can manipulate on your profile to help more people connect with you, and increase the odds of people accepting your requests when you reach out.

First, make sure your profile picture is visible to those who are not connected to you. One of the main reasons I may hold off on sending or accepting connections, is if I can’t see their profile picture. It communicates to me that they’re either not real, or not that engaged on the platform.

Next, you should also show your summary/bio info to those who aren’t connected with you. How are others going to be interested in networking with you if they don’t know anything about you? Do you think recruiters would like to read your bio/summary? Yes!

Lastly, show your education and work history to those you’re not connected with too. The point of LinkedIn is to expand your professional network, so don’t be shy – turn on the “show education and work history” button in your settings.

Bonus Tips:

Make sure you have a good headshot profile picture AND a banner pic (not the standard LI one). As I mentioned before, if you don’t have a picture, people are less likely to connect with you.
