TECH TRUCK ASSEMBLY
All parts of the Tech Truck will be shipped directly to Tema, Ghana’s main port. The hardest
element of importing the parts is importing the truck itself, mainly due to its size. However,
roll-on-roll-off shipping methods will work (quote from A1-Autotransport).
Once in Tema, the individual elements of the Tech Truck will be assembled. The main work that
is required is to install the solar panels and paint the truck. These are both simple to do and will
not cost very much. The rest, such as internet, devices, and generators, can be stored inside the
truck. One auto-body shop that is equipped for these modifications is Harena Auto, a Candian
company with a location in Tema.
Then, the truck will be driven out to its assigned location.

TECH TRUCK 27
M A X I M I Z I N G E F F I C I E N C Y
A LIBRARY-STYLE APPROACH
IS MORE EFFECTIVE
Say there are 500 readers, and one book costs $1. That means that getting each of them
individual books would cost $500 dollars. Alternatively, you could buy 50 books for $50 and have
a community-based, library approach where books are shared. They achieve the same result -
in the end, everyone will have read a book. However, the library approach is just more effective.
This is especially true in regards to solutions like ours, where the components could potentially
be very costly. If you compare the costs: Recall that one truck of ours will cost ~ $331,117 over the
next five years, while giving satellite internet and devices to everyone will cost ~ $950,000 in the
same time period. Creating coaxial cable lines will cost ~ $132,000 per mile, so that is not a viable
solution either.

TECH TRUCK 28
I N F R A S T R U C T U R E C O M P A R I S O N
TRUCKS ARE MORE
EFFECTIVE THAN BUILDINGS
When crafting our solution, we considered fabricating a building or permanent station. We
found that our solution trumps building brick and mortar centers. The key reasons why include
mobility, access, and speed of implementation.
1. Mobility: if something were to malfunction, or the location were to change, the truck
could simply drive to the new location.
2. Access: the roads are not all paved, and if women do not go to computers, the
computers must go to them, even in the far reaches of rural Ghana.
3. Speed of implementation: Buildings take time and money to build and set up. In a five
year time span, there is no time for that. Conversely, trucks can be fitted multiple times
in a faster and cheaper manner.

T W O D E P L O Y M E N T O P T I O N S
BOTH STATIONARY AND
MOBILE CAPABILITIES
We’ve discussed the cost and the core components of what makes our trucks. But how will they
be deployed?
Ideally, we would deploy them in such a manner that has the most impact. The two options we
found were either keeping the truck stationary - that is, one truck will stay at one location - or
mobile, in which case a truck will cycle throughout multiple towns.
It’s a case of either going for wide impact or going for deep, concentrated impact. Either way
is an acceptable way to approach the challenge, and as such, we included statistics for both.
