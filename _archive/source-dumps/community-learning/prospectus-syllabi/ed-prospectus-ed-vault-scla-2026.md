High Agency and Durable Skills

At the SCLA, our mission is clear: equip our members to succeed in the new era of work. This is the driving force behind everything we do.

Today’s workforce demands new skills and a high-agency mindset. Without them, organizations, institutions, and ultimately workers will be left behind.

In 2026, our learning and engagement mission centers on transforming how learners operate and prepare for the evolving workforce. We focus on cultivating high agency and future-facing skill sets that propel members forward in every area of their lives.

Operating Principles framing

To make this real, we are intentionally and strategically shifting how we design, deliver, and evaluate learning. We are establishing clear Operating Principles that define our education performance standard.

These principles are not just aspirational. They are embedded in:

What we create (programs, tools, events, communications)

How we create and work together as a team

How we measure success, impact, and performance

They serve as our daily checklist and our long-term bar: if we are not building high-agency, future-ready learners, we are not done.

Definition (SCLA language)

High-agency individuals demonstrate strong personal initiative, creative problem-solving, and an unwavering belief that they can influence their own outcomes. They are resourceful, act without waiting for permission, and focus on getting what they want in ways that bypass traditional roadblocks or “default” paths.

High agency also has a contrarian edge: instead of following the standard script (submit a résumé into a portal and hope), they design creative, direct ways to achieve their goals—like building a tailored project for a hiring manager or finding a warm introduction to the CTO instead of competing in a pile of 1,000 applications.

In a world where intelligence is increasingly commodified by AI, high agency is the differentiator. Tools can generate answers, but they cannot supply drive, mission, or the willingness to bend reality toward a vision. People without their own goals, ideas, or creativity are easier to automate; high-agency people are not.

Opposite of high agency

The opposite of high agency is passive compliance.

Low-agency individuals:

Have no mission of their own and rely on others to define “success.”

Struggle to deviate from the script, syllabus, or job description they’ve been handed.

Wait for instructions, default paths, and external validation.

Risk becoming “background characters” in their careers, more easily replaced by automation or AI.

Core characteristics of high-agency learners

You already listed strong traits; here’s a polished version you can use as a rubric or slide.

Proactive problem-solving
High-agency learners treat obstacles as puzzles, not dead ends. They generate options, test alternatives, and pull in the right people or resources instead of stopping at the first “no.”

Ownership
They take responsibility for actions, outcomes, and learning gaps. They don’t blame luck, institutions, or “the system” as the final word; they ask, “What can I do next?”

Action-oriented
They prioritize movement over perfection. They ship drafts, run experiments, and iterate, instead of waiting for ideal conditions or getting stuck in endless planning.

Independent
They act on their own initiative, even when it means diverging from stereotypes, family expectations, or the “safe” route peers are choosing.

Goal-focused
They know what they want—or are actively clarifying it—and align their choices, projects, and relationships toward those goals.

How high agency works (model for your programs)

You can present this as a simple 3-part model: mindset → posture → action.

Internal mindset
High agency starts as a belief: “My choices genuinely matter.” Learners see themselves as actors in their own story, not just as reactors to systems or circumstances.

Internal posture
This belief becomes a stance: “I take responsibility, regardless of the odds.” It’s a commitment to ownership even when the situation is unfair, ambiguous, or hard.

External action
The posture shows up in concrete behavior: asking for what they want, escalating issues constructively, designing their own opportunities, and making unconventional moves (e.g., asking for a better table, emailing the decision-maker, proposing a new role).

This is where your programming lives: not just teaching what high agency is, but repeatedly giving students chances to practice this cycle with feedback.

Durable skills in the AI era (how it connects)

You can bridge high agency to “durable skills” like this:

AI can handle information retrieval and even parts of analysis, but it cannot replace human judgment, initiative, ethics, or relational intelligence.

Over-reliance on AI can lead to “cognitive offloading,” where people stop practicing critical thinking and independent analysis.

High-agency learners use AI as a power tool, not a crutch. They pair AI with:

Critical thinking (deciding what to ask, what to trust, what to build next)

Communication (framing ideas, persuading humans, building buy-in)

Adaptability (pivoting when conditions change or when the AI outputs are wrong)

Ethical awareness (choosing what should be done, not just what can be done)
