[▶ VIDEO] How to Use the Career Mapping Tool

Link to Video: How to Use the Career Mapping Tool

Part 1 – Start your career map

Open the Career Mapping Tool from your course or dashboard.

On the landing screen, look for the section titled "Map your career." This page explains that the tool will help you build a personalized roadmap based on your goals and preferences.

Click the button to begin mapping your career (for example, "Start," "Get started," or similar, depending on your LMS setup).

Part 2 – Choose your career approach

On the "Choose your career paths" screen, select up to three career approaches that fit your aspirations and values. The options may include: Traditional path, Fast track, Entrepreneurial, Work‑life balance, Location independent, and Social impact.

Click on the tile(s) for the approaches that resonate most with you. You can select one, two, or three.

Once you've made your choices, click the button to continue (for example, "Next" or "Continue").

Part 3 – Complete your profile and experience

On the profile section, fill in the requested information about yourself so the tool can tailor your career map. This may include your name, preferred industry, and general background.

In the "Your experience" area, upload or select a resume if requested. This helps the tool align your map with your current skills and experience.

Part 4 – Customize your selected paths

For each career approach you selected, you can optionally add more details such as target industries and specific goals.

When you're finished customizing, click the button to generate or update your career map.

Part 5 – Generate or regenerate your career map

When you select "Regenerate career map" or a similar option, a confirmation message will appear explaining that your old career approaches and jobs will be removed

If you are sure you want to create a new map, click "Regenerate"; if not, click "Cancel."

Part 6 – Review your generated career map

When your map is ready, you'll see a summary that tells you how many paths and jobs were generated (for example, "You have generated your career map with one path containing three jobs").

Click "Show more" or similar links to expand details about each path and job if you want a deeper look.

Part 7 – Select your preferred path

On the "Choose your career path" screen, review the list of generated paths and their quick stats (such as number of roles, industry, or focus like "goal‑oriented" or "work‑life balance").

Confirm your selection by clicking "Select this path," "Select and continue," or the equivalent button.

Part 8 – Explore your career map details

On the Career Map overview page, review your personalized roadmap, including the time frame (for example, 2026–2030), preferred industry, and five‑year goal.

Repeat this for each job in your path to understand how responsibilities and expectations grow over time.

[▶ VIDEO] Lesson · Using the Career Map tool to boost your next decision

Now that you have used the tool and found some new career ideas, it's time to turn those career criteria into a side-by-side view and put your career options into practice.   The Career Map is a simple way to see your options side by side instead of agonizing over them one at a time. Plot each option you are considering on two axes:

The value isn't the score. It's what you notice while scoring — "huh, the safe option is weak on everything I said mattered most." That noticing is the point.

Horizontal — Personal fit & energy: How well does this match what you are good at and what energizes you? (Use your themes from the previous activity.)

Vertical — Long-term value/career capital: How much does this build skills, connections, credentials, and options you will value later?

Four zones emerge:

Prioritize (high fit, high value): pursue these first.

Explore (high value, uncertain fit): promising but unproven — worth a cheap test.

Enjoy / Side (high fit, lower value): great energy, keep as a backup or a side pursuit.

Park (low fit, low value): set these aside without guilt.

Seeing your options mapped out does two things at once: it widens your frame (you consider more than one path), and it applies consistent criteria to all of them.
