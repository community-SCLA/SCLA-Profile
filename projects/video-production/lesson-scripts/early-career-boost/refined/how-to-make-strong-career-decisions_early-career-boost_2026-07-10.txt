What might a good career decision process actually look like? A common approach is to make a pro and con list. But you can do much better. Pro and con lists make it easy to give too much weight to an unimportant factor, and they do not push you toward the more powerful methods that genuinely improve the quality of a decision.

First, a reality check. Career decisions usually involve real uncertainty, so if you feel stressed or anxious about one, that is completely normal. This process will not make your decision easy. But if you work through it, you are far less likely to miss something important, and far more likely to take the best next step you can.

Here is the surprising part: most people do not use any process at all. That is a mistake, because we are not naturally good at complex decisions. If you just go with your gut, you will probably overlook something. This process will not tell you what to do — but it will put you in the best possible position to decide. And because it is a repeatable loop, you can run it for any choice: which internship, which major, which offer, which next move.

Step one: clarify the decision. Get clear on exactly what you are choosing, and by when. Vague decisions produce vague answers.

Step two: widen your options. One of the most serious mistakes people make is considering too few options — and research suggests even one extra option can improve how satisfied you are with the outcome. So before you narrow anything down, push yourself to list more paths than feels comfortable. Ask "what else?" at least twice.

Step three: set your criteria. Write down the three to seven factors that truly matter to you — things like fit, energy, career capital, impact, satisfaction, and any practical constraints. Then rate your options against those criteria, not against your mood.

Step four: test your assumptions. You rarely need certainty. You need to lower the risk on the one assumption that matters most. So find your biggest uncertainty and run a cheap experiment to reduce it — a single informational interview, or a weekend project, can do a lot.

Step five: decide and commit, then review. Make the call, act on it, and set a date to check back in. Trust your gut as one input, sleep on big decisions, but do not mistake a mood for a verdict. Good decisions are revisited, not carved in stone.

Because this is a loop and not a one-time event, it works with the fact that you will learn things you cannot possibly know right now. That is the whole idea. And once you know how to make one good decision, you can use that same skill again and again.
