Everything you have done so far — your criteria, your energy themes, your mapped options — comes together in one short, personal artifact: a Career Purpose Statement. Think of it as a north star you can actually use.

A Career Purpose Statement is just one or two sentences. It is not a corporate mission statement, and it is not permanent. It is a working answer to a simple question: what am I trying to do with my work right now, and why? Specific enough to guide a real decision, and honest enough to actually be yours.

A good one usually blends three things. First, what energizes you — the kind of work you noticed yourself coming alive in. Second, who or what you want to help — your "helps others" criterion, made specific. And third, the kind of value you want to build — the strengths and skills you are growing toward.

There is a simple structure that works. I want to use these strengths, to help this person or create this outcome, because this is why it matters to me. Notice what is not in there: no grand claims, no buzzwords. Just direction, contribution, and a reason. Yours should sound like you.

A strong statement has a few qualities worth aiming for. It feels inspiring and true to you. It is driven by real energy, not obligation. It gives you a mission — a real focus. It is something you would commit to for the next several years. And it is brief — easy to remember, and easy to repeat.

It can feel awkward to write, and that is completely normal. You are putting words to something that was fuzzy five minutes ago. So aim for honest and rough over polished and empty. Pull up your notes, write three quick versions, and pick the one that rings truest. You do not need to get it perfect, because you can revise it every single time you run your decision loop.

That is the confidence half of clarity and confidence. It does not come from having certainty about the future. It comes from having a method you can return to — and a purpose, in your own words, to point it at.
