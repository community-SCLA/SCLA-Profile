In the last lesson, you built a picture of what generally makes work good — the five conditions worth looking for. This lesson gets specific to you: what actually gives you energy, and what drains it.

That's a more reliable question than "what am I passionate about?" As we said, passion is vague and high-pressure. Energy is observable. And you already have data. You've had classes, jobs, projects, volunteer roles, group work. Some of it left you buzzing. Some of it left you flat. That pattern is a signal.

So keep two columns in mind: what energizes you, and what drains you. That's the frame for the worksheet attached to this lesson — the Energizing Work Worksheet.

Here's what you're not doing. You're not looking for a single lightning-bolt answer. You're looking for patterns — two or three recurring themes are more than enough to start pointing a direction. And you're not looking for a job title. You're looking for the ingredients of work that fits you, the raw material for direction.

When you're early in your career, this is genuinely hard to name. You've tried different classes, jobs, and projects, but the patterns aren't always obvious. Looking at several real experiences side by side — and using an AI coach to analyze them — helps you see them more clearly. You'll start to notice the kinds of tasks that feel most engaging, the ways you naturally like to contribute — organizing, creating, helping, solving, analyzing — and early clues about roles or paths worth exploring next.

Think of this as a structured conversation about what actually gives you energy, not what you think you "should" enjoy. The goal isn't to lock you into one path. It's to give you better language and insight, so future career decisions feel grounded instead of random.

Now it's your turn — and this is also a great way to practice using AI. You're going to have a short conversation with your AI Energy Coach. Open it in the next section. Paste the starter prompt, or write your own, then answer its follow-up questions honestly. It'll ask you about moments you felt most engaged, capable, or in flow — one question at a time — and after a few, it reflects back the two or three patterns it hears. When you're done, save that summary to your notes.

The more specific and honest your examples, the more useful the reflection. Describe real experiences — classes, jobs, projects, roles — and how each one made you feel. You can end the conversation at any time by saying "end conversation."
