===== CURRICULUM =====

Skills for the AI-era Future

Learn the human skills that demonstrate your value as AI reshapes the work.

It is easy to feel anxious about starting a career while headlines announce that AI is coming for every job. Here is a calmer, more accurate frame: AI is very good at tasks, and careers are made of far more than tasks. The skills that stay valuable are the ones that are hardest to automate and that make you the person who can direct AI well, rather than compete with it.

The durable, human-centered skills to invest in early:

Judgment and decision-making. Exactly what you built in Module 3. Deciding what is worth doing is harder to automate than doing it.

Communication and persuasion. Explaining ideas clearly, building trust, and moving people to act.

Relationships and collaboration. Leadership, empathy, and working across teams — deeply human, deeply valued.

Learning how to learn. In a fast-changing field, the ability to pick up new tools quickly beats mastery of any one tool.

Working with AI. Knowing how to prompt, check, and combine AI outputs with your own judgment is quickly becoming a baseline professional skill.

None of these require you to out-compute a machine. They require you to be a thoughtful, capable human who uses powerful tools well.

Script

Hi.  This is a bonus module for anyone interested in learning how AI is now integrated into the career decision-making and mapping process.  Ten years ago, we told a lot of people they should learn to code. This worked out well for those who did: software engineering jobs grew rapidly for years afterward.  But today, the question of whether to learn to code is much more ambiguous.

Although many economists still dismiss the possibility, experts in the technology itself believe it will be possible to eventually build AI and robotic systems that will be able to do every economically important task more efficiently than humans.

Many assume this will drive down wages. But the reality is more complicated. Even if wages do eventually fall, that'll likely be preceded by a period in which automation generates a huge amount of wealth, driving wages up on average. During that period, as AI drives down the value of skills it has mastered, it'll drive up the value of skills it hasn't.

As a job-seeker, your best strategy is to learn skills that AI will increasingly make you valuable, staying one step ahead of automation. It's hard to predict exactly what those skills will be, but we think an even bigger mistake would be to ignore what's happening.

You shouldn't be training as a lawyer, accountant, financial analyst, actuary, writer, translator, or graphic designer right now without giving serious thought to the effects of AI.

Just as in 2015 it was possible to say that coding and data science were sectors with huge growth potential, it's possible to make similar (if not certain) bets now about the skills that will put you in the best position over the next 10 years. That's what we'll explore here.

Which skills will be most valuable in the future?

The skills that will be most valuable in the future are those that are useful in the most impactful jobs; fast to learn relative to their value; transferable; and that AI is likely to make more, rather than less, valuable.

The skills that will increase in value due to AI are those that are hard to automate (because they are messy, involve doing work over a long time-horizon, or require human judgment), needed for AI deployment, or can be used to produce things people always want more of.

Some especially transferable skills that are valuable in any job:

Deploying AI to solve real-world problems

Taking care of yourself and your mental health (sleep, exercise, mental health)

Personal effectiveness and agency

Prioritization, forecasting, and decision-making

Social skills

Learning how to learn

Most valuable transferable work skills:

Leadership and management

Most valuable specialist skills:

Machine learning research and engineering

Four types of skills likely to increase in value in an age of AI

We've reviewed all the economic literature and recent data we could find on AI automation, and combined that with some theory about how AI works to identify the following four skill categories more likely to increase in value. Which skills to focus on is a moving target, but you can use these categories to evaluate your options at the time. You'll want to look for skills that are valuable today and that satisfy at least two of the four categories.

1. Skills that are hard to automate

LLMs like ChatGPT are trained to predict patterns in (primarily) internet data. This makes them excellent at tasks that involve pattern matching, recall, and recombination of that data. That turns out to be a lot of tasks.

In 2017, experts predicted that social skills and creativity would be among the least susceptible to automation. Today, therapy is a popular use of AI, and it's also surprisingly good at certain types of idea generation, creating visuals in novel combinations of artistic styles, and writing poetry that most can't distinguish from human-authored work.

On the other hand, AI remains poor at tasks not present in its training data. The internet contains huge amounts of text, but there's no equivalent store of data describing physical movement. Creating this data will require building physical robots with cutting-edge sensors and having them move around the real world. So, unlike previous waves of automation, white-collar workers are likely to be affected before many blue-collar ones.

The physical tasks that will resist automation the longest will be those that require the most complex movement, are performed in unpredictable environments, and require the highest reliability, because these factors increase the amount of data needed to describe the task. Driving seems close to cracked, with Waymo operating fully self-driving cars in multiple cities and growing over 10% per month, but something like surgery or plumbing seems significantly further away.

But consider a task like building a company: it involves making judgment calls with no obviously correct answers, and success is determined over many years. It's much harder to get reinforcement learning to work for a task like this. Other examples might include starting a new community, directing a frontier research project, having a conceptual insight, or setting an organizational strategy. All of these require skills that aren't clearly described within internet data, but are also:

Messy — they lack clearly defined instructions and measurable outcomes.

Long-horizon — they take time to implement and measure success.

This is why AI can solve maths problems most PhDs would struggle with, but, as of 2025, it is still worse than most 10-year-olds at playing Pokemon, a game which is open-ended and unfolds over days.

The following are some categories of tasks suggested by economists who study automation in which people might demand a 'human-in-the-loop:

Legal liability: Someone needs to be legally responsible for important decisions, such as chartered engineers or court lawyers.

High reliability is required: AI systems make weird mistakes, so people will want human oversight, such as historians reviewing AI-generated research, controlling expensive industrial machinery, or military leadership.

Professional protections: Doctors and lawyers control certifications and form powerful lobbies that could block AI applications.

Preference for human touch: Many people prefer human service providers for certain services, perhaps as a luxury, such as nannies, artists with compelling stories, and religious leaders.

Physical presence needed: Many roles require someone to be physically present, and in some cases, people might not be willing to fully hand these responsibilities over to robots for a long time, such as in police or teaching roles.

Institutional inertia: Many organizations will be slow to adopt AI, keeping humans in important jobs longer than makes sense, including government agencies and monopolistic companies.

2. Skills needed for AI development

Generative AI startups are growing fast, even compared to the previous generation of software as a service (SaaS) companies, themselves famous for their speedy growth:

As AIs move from being reactive chatbots into proactive agents, the skills required to direct these 'digital workers' will become incredibly valuable.

You will be able to use these workers to rapidly build and iterate on new products, but you could just as easily have them help you set up a nonprofit on a shoestring budget, search for new cancer drugs, or provide expert medical advice 24/7 anywhere in the world. Key skills could include:

Spotting problems and deciding what to focus on

Understanding different AI models and designing around their weaknesses

Writing clear project specifications for AI agents to follow

Understanding what users really want

Designing systems to manage AI workers, including error-checking

Coordinating with people and bearing responsibility for results

Other complementary skills involve knowledge of the fields needed for AI scale-up, such as AI software and hardware development; robotics development, manufacture, and maintenance; the construction of datacentres and factories required for these; and cybersecurity.

3. Skills that produce things people could use far more of

I only need to file my taxes once a year, so if AI can do it more cheaply, I will spend less on my accountant. I'm not going to start filing taxes more often with the money saved. In contrast, as we saw, the arrival of ride-sharing platforms made taxis much cheaper and easier to use, and as a result, people took way more rides.

There are many other areas where, as what's on offer gets better and cheaper, people use them more and more. As people get richer, they spend more of their income on healthcare, suggesting that even if technology made healthcare more efficient, people would simply use far more of it.

AI automation will also likely create a huge amount of wealth, potentially concentrated in the top 1%, who own most of the capital and therefore control the AI systems that automate. Increased income inequality will spike demand for luxury goods.

4. Specialized skills that are hard for others to learn

Consider a job like being a server at a fancy restaurant. People often eat out more as they get wealthier, and this is the sort of physical, social-skills-heavy job where people might retain a strong preference for a human touch, so I expect there to be plenty of jobs like this going forward.

However, it's also a job that other people can enter relatively quickly. The skills that will see the largest increases in value will be those that take time for the labor market to respond.

This favors learning specialized skills that match your unique strengths. For example, construction work is a complex physical job that will be harder to automate. However, a construction worker who learns a trade, such as becoming an electrician, and specializes in data centers is more likely to become a critical bottleneck and see the value of their skills increase much faster. In fact, electrician wages in Virginia, where many datacentres are being built, are already 37% above the national average. So which specific skills will be most valuable in the future?

Which skills will be most valuable in the future?

Broadly speaking, the most valuable skills will be those that are most needed in the categories mentioned earlier.  Spending eight years in medical school, for instance, is a much riskier proposition today.

Another quality that makes skills more valuable is their transferability across different jobs and problems. Management is a relatively transferable skill because basically every organization needs managers, so even if one industry disappears, or you decide you want to switch into a totally different area, your skills are more likely to remain useful. Become an expert in early Roman portraiture, however, and your options are more limited.

Some skills are so transferable that they're useful in basically every career. Here are some that seem likely to increase in value in the future and which we consider especially useful for impactful career paths.

Although they're not all the classic 'work skills' you'd list on your LinkedIn profile, they're often more worth learning, because they'll make you more effective at everything else you want to do. You can start practicing them in whatever job you're doing now.

Deploying AI to solve real-world problems: If I had just one piece of advice right now, it would be to learn to use AI to accomplish real work.   This advice has already become a bit of a cliche, but it is still often not applied.

Taking care of yourself: Ambitious people often neglect their own well-being. This can make them burn out and ultimately be less successful. Taking care of yourself starts with the basics: getting enough sleep, exercising regularly, eating well, and maintaining your close friendships. Building these habits can have a big impact on your day-to-day energy and happiness.

Personal effectiveness and agency: It's often possible to significantly increase your productivity by developing good habits, like setting daily goals and learning to run meetings. These skills are relatively quick to improve and help you in every job. On a more advanced level, if you can become the type of person who can work on difficult, ill-defined problems over many years, that'll be of huge value. And this is something AI is likely to continue to struggle with.

Prioritization, forecasting, and decision-making: If you want to do good, these skills are probably even more important because some projects have much more impact than others, and these skills are needed to identify what's best.

Social skills: Every job involves working with others at least to some degree, and the importance of social skills continues to increase over time. Looking forward, even though AI is good at certain types of social interaction, people will still want relationships with other people. Social skills are also a core component of leadership and management and are vital in many of the most impactful roles, such as policy and communications.

Learning how to learn: Being able to learn quickly is useful almost no matter what you want to do, but as technology changes faster, the ability to rapidly retrain becomes ever more valuable. AI has also meant you can get basically free 24/7 private tutoring on almost any skill, which many people say enables them to learn much faster than before.

Some other skills are harder to learn, but true expertise seems likely to be among the last things to get automated, and experts of many kinds will be required to oversee AI systems. If you really want to up your game, here are some other skills to dive into:

Machine learning research and engineering (i.e. AI Safety Research, or AI Communications or Operations)

AI governance and strategy (i.e. AI Policy or Space Governance)

Cyber and information security

Expertise in an emerging power: China is likely to play a crucial role in shaping the coming decades, but decision makers in the West tend to understand China poorly and often fail to coordinate with it to solve global problems.
More broadly, the world is transitioning from an era of US dominance to a multipolar system, including countries like India, Russia, and Saudi Arabia, expanding their spheres of influence. These dynamics will affect the development of many issues going forward.

Expertise in AI hardware: AI chips may turn out to be the oil of the next century. Whichever country has the best and the most chips will be able to deploy the most effective AI systems and could therefore wield the most economic power.

Here are several other valuable areas of specialist expertise, though they're less targeted at the most crucial gaps, and in some cases, less transferable:

Finance, especially the understanding of growth and automation, though also its general toolkit

International relations, security studies, and public policy

Robotics development and maintenance (as well as the construction of datacentres and factories), since they often become huge bottlenecks, making them valuable skills, especially for earning to give

Engineering, especially in healthcare and manufacturing

Synthetic biology and bioengineering, especially in agriculture and healthcare

Applied mathematics, since those skills are useful in many other fields

Certain branches of philosophy, sociology, such as moral philosophy and philosophy of mind

Certain areas of history, such as public policy, economic history, macrohistory, and social change and important ideas

Finally, the value of skills used in jobs that require a physical presence or involve complex physical manipulation — such as those used in law enforcement, construction, teaching, and surgery — and the skills used to provide luxury services or where a human touch is highly valued — like mediator, conference organiser, artist, or bespoke craftsperson — will probably keep pace with the rest of the economy, though won't obviously outpace it.

Wrap-Up

But in every case, remember what the history of automation tells us about its likely future. While most people assume automation drives down wages, that's only true when it's nearly 100%. Until then, automation drives down the value of some skills, but drives up the value of others. The aim isn't to find a single job that will never be automated, but rather to ride the wave, moving into the skills that become most valuable at each time.
