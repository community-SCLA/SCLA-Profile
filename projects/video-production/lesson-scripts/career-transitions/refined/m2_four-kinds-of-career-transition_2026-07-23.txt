When you're thinking about a career move, it's tempting to jump straight to tactics. Should I update my resume? Apply to that role? Go back to school? Those questions matter — but they're not the first step. Before you plan the move, name the kind of move you're making. Each kind of transition has a different rhythm, different risks, and different tactics.

In this lesson, we'll use a simple matrix with two axes. Horizontally: how big the change is, from adjacent to dramatic. Vertically: what triggers it — chosen or forced. Together, those axes give us four transition types: the Growth Pivot, the Reinvention, the Redirect, and the Rebuild. Let's walk through each one.

First, the Growth Pivot — a chosen, adjacent change. You stay in a similar industry or function, but step into a meaningfully new direction or scope. Think of a senior marketer who becomes head of brand strategy on a bigger stage. The domain is familiar; the responsibility, complexity, and visibility grow. The rhythm here feels like expansion — more leadership, more impact. Your tactics: deepen your expertise, expand your network, and signal you're ready for larger roles.

Second, the Reinvention — a chosen, dramatic change. You deliberately build a new identity in a new field: a corporate lawyer becoming a fitness executive, say. Reinventions look bold from the outside, but they're rarely a single leap. They usually rest on years of adjacent moves — skills, taste, and relationships — that make the new path possible. Vera Wang's story shows it clearly. She was a competitive figure skater, then a fashion editor for sixteen years, then a design director at Ralph Lauren — before she founded her bridal label at forty. The pivot looks dramatic. The accumulation was adjacent.

Third, the Redirect — a forced, adjacent change. Something external happens — a layoff, a restructuring, an industry slowdown — but your skills still transfer well. Picture someone laid off from a shrinking function who lands a similar role at a healthier company. The trigger was forced; the path stays close to what they've been doing. This one feels like course correction, not reinvention. Your tactics: deal honestly with the emotional impact, then focus on where your existing strengths can move into safer or more promising contexts.

Fourth, the Rebuild — a forced, dramatic change. A life event or disruptive shift — a health event, caregiving responsibilities, a geographic move, or technological change — requires a fundamentally new path. Imagine a role AI or automation is erasing, or a personal event that reshapes your priorities and capacity.

Let's be clear about something: forced does not mean failed. Research on career grief and involuntary transitions shows forced changes are just as capable of producing what scholars call adaptive identity development — the disruption becomes the catalyst for meaningful growth. What determines that isn't whether you chose the transition. It's how you respond: whether you allow space for grief, seek support, look for ways to transfer your strengths into new directions, and experiment with new identities instead of clinging to old ones.

One more note on dramatic transitions. From the outside, they look like single bold leaps. In reality, they almost always involve adjacent steps that quietly accumulate capacity. Zoom out and you can usually see the trail — small projects, side interests, courses, relationships, and experiments that make the big leap less sudden than it appears.

So what does this mean for you? First, name your transition type. Are you in a Growth Pivot? Planning a Reinvention? Navigating a Redirect? Working through a Rebuild? Second, match your tactics to it. Growth Pivots need stronger signals of readiness and visibility in your current lane. Reinventions require learning, experimentation, and building credibility in a new space. Redirects call for emotional processing and strategic re-targeting of your existing strengths. Rebuilds benefit from support, patience, and a focus on transferable skills and values.

Naming your transition type doesn't solve everything. But it gives you a clearer map of what you're navigating — and a clearer map makes it easier to choose your next step.
