There's a moment in every career transition where the internal work has to meet the external world. You've done the identity audit. You've built your carry-forward inventory. You've started running experiments. Now you're about to talk to a hiring manager, a former colleague, a former mentor — and they're going to ask, "Wait, what are you doing now?"

That moment is where most pivots either land or unravel.

Herminia Ibarra and Roxana Barbulescu, in their research on career transitions, found something worth pausing on. They studied whether hiring communities grant someone's new identity claim — in other words, whether people actually accept you as a legitimate member of the new field. And what they found is that the single strongest predictor of a granted identity is what they call narrative coherence.

That means the story you tell about your change makes sense. It connects past and future in a way a listener can follow — without contradiction, without apology, without hiding what came before.

A good pivot story has three parts.

First, the backstory: what you built and accomplished in your prior career. This is where your credibility lives. You don't minimize it. You don't apologize for it. You claim it.

Second, the turning point: what changed, shifted, or became possible. This is where you explain the pivot without self-deprecation. Notice the shift in framing — not "I was miserable and had to escape," but "three things converged that made this next direction the clear one." Same event. Very different story.

Third, the forward case: why your specific combination of background and new direction creates unique value. This is where you claim the advantage your pivot creates. The finance director turned product manager doesn't lead with "I'm learning product." She leads with "I'm a product manager who can read a P&L like most of my peers can't."

Now watch for three narrative failure modes.

First, apologizing for the pivot. "I know this seems like a big jump…" Cut that. It signals a lack of conviction to the very people you need to convince.

Second, framing the old career as a mistake. Even if you personally feel that way — and many people do — that framing tanks the story. Everything you've done contributed to what you're becoming. Say that.

Third, expecting the listener to make the connections. They won't. If your old work relates to your new direction, you have to name the bridge. Explicitly.
