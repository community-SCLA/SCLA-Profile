Career identity is layered. The role you held is only the top layer. Underneath sit the routines, the relationships, the sense of expertise, the way others saw you, and the internal story you told yourself about what you were doing and why it mattered.

Here's the good news: you don't have to let go of all of it at once. Some of those layers are ready to be released. Some aren't yet. And some don't need to be released at all — they only need to be reframed. That's what this audit is for: sorting your identity layer by layer, so you know exactly what you're carrying and what you're setting down.

There are five layers to audit.

The first is your Role or Title — what you were called. It's often the easiest layer to release on the outside, and harder on the inside than most people expect.

The second is your Routines and Environment — the day-to-day rhythms, the physical or digital workspace, the meetings, the calendar.

The third is your Relationships — colleagues, clients, mentors, the professional community you built. Some of your people will travel forward with you; some belong to that specific chapter.

The fourth is your Sense of Expertise — the confidence of being genuinely good at something. This is often the hardest layer to release, because releasing it means stepping back into a beginner's mindset.

And the fifth is your Internal Story — the narrative you told yourself about who you were and why your work mattered.

So how do you actually run the audit? Take each layer, one at a time, and ask three questions. First: what is genuinely ending here? Second: what am I grieving, even if I chose this? And third: what deserves to be honored before I move on?

That last question matters more than it looks, because honoring an ending is where ritual comes in. Career grief is real — and small, structured rituals actively support the ending phase. A written letter to the old career. A curated list of what it gave you. A quiet dinner with a colleague from that chapter. These are not soft, optional extras. They are evidence-based practices for closing the ending phase cleanly.

So work the audit layer by layer: name what's ending, name what you're grieving, and decide what deserves to be honored before you move on. That's how you close this chapter well — and step into the next one ready.
