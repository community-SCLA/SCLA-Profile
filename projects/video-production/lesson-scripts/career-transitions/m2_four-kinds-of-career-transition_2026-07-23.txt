When you’re thinking about a career move, it’s tempting to jump straight to tactics: “Should I update my resume?” “Should I apply to that role?” “Should I go back to school?”

Those questions matter—but they’re not the first step.

Before you plan the move, you need to name the kind of move you’re making. Each kind of transition has a different rhythm, different risks, and different tactics.

[On screen: Title card appears — “Four Kinds of Career Transition.” Underneath, a simple 2×2 grid with faint labels.]

In this lesson, we’ll look at a simple matrix with two axes:

horizontally: how big the change is — from adjacent to dramatic

vertically: what triggers the change — chosen versus forced.

[On screen: 2×2 matrix animates in. Horizontal axis: “Adjacent” → “Dramatic.” Vertical axis: “Chosen” → “Forced.” Four quadrants appear.]

Together, these axes give us four transition types:

The Growth Pivot

The Reinvention

The Redirect

The Rebuild.

Let’s walk through each one.

First: The Growth Pivot.

This is a chosen, adjacent change. You stay in a similar industry or function, but step into a meaningfully new direction or scope.

Think of a senior marketer who becomes head of brand strategy at a bigger stage. The domain is familiar, but the level of responsibility, complexity, and visibility grow.

[On screen: Top-left quadrant highlights. Card label: “Growth Pivot – chosen + adjacent.” One-line description: “Same lane, bigger stage or sharper focus.” Example: “Senior marketer → Head of brand strategy.”]

The rhythm here often feels like expansion: more leadership, more complexity, more impact. The main tactics involve deepening expertise, expanding your network, and signalling readiness for larger roles.

Second: The Reinvention.

This is a chosen, dramatic change. You deliberately build a new identity in a new field.

Classic examples include a corporate lawyer becoming a fitness executive, or Vera Wang’s move into bridal design at age 40.

[On screen: Top-right quadrant highlights. Card label: “Reinvention – chosen + dramatic.” One-line description: “Deliberately creating a new professional identity.” Example: “Corporate lawyer → Fitness executive.”]

Reinventions look bold from the outside, but they’re rarely a single leap. They usually rest on years of adjacent moves—skills, taste, and relationships—that make the new path possible.

Vera Wang’s story is a good illustration. She was a competitive figure skater, then a fashion editor for 16 years, then a design director at Ralph Lauren—before she founded her bridal label at 40. The pivot looks dramatic. The accumulation was adjacent.

Third: The Redirect.

This is a forced, adjacent change. Something external happens—like a layoff, a restructuring, or an industry slowdown—but your skills still transfer well.

Picture someone laid off from a shrinking function who lands a similar role at a healthier company. The trigger was forced, but the path itself remains close to what they’ve been doing.

[On screen: Bottom-left quadrant highlights. Card label: “Redirect – forced + adjacent.” One-line description: “External event, skills largely transfer.” Example: “Layoff → similar role in stronger organization.”]

The rhythm here often feels like course correction rather than reinvention. The key tactics involve dealing with the emotional impact of the disruption, then focusing on where your existing strengths can move into safer or more promising contexts.

Fourth: The Rebuild.

This is a forced, dramatic change. A life event or disruptive shift—like a health event, caregiving responsibilities, a geographic move, or technological change—requires a fundamentally new path.

You might imagine a role heavily impacted by AI or automation where the job itself is disappearing, or a personal event that reshapes your priorities and capacity.

[On screen: Bottom-right quadrant highlights. Card label: “Rebuild – forced + dramatic.” One-line description: “Disruption demands a fundamentally new path.” Example: “AI disruption ending a role → new purpose-driven work.”]

It’s important to be clear about something here: forced does not mean failed.

Research on career grief and involuntary transitions shows that forced career changes are just as capable of producing what scholars call adaptive identity development—where the disruption becomes the catalyst for meaningful growth.

The determining factor is not whether you chose the transition. It’s how you respond to it:

whether you allow space for grief

whether you seek support

whether you look for ways to transfer strengths into new directions

whether you experiment with new identities instead of clinging to old ones.

[On screen: Note card appears: “Forced ≠ failed. Response matters.”]

Likewise, a note on dramatic transitions: from the outside, they often look like single bold leaps. In reality, they almost always involve a series of adjacent steps that quietly accumulate capacity over time.

When you zoom out, you can usually see a trail: small projects, side interests, courses, relationships, and experiments that make the “big leap” less sudden than it appears.

[On screen: Timeline graphic showing “adjacent steps” leading toward a visible pivot.]

So what does this mean for you, practically?

First, name your transition type:

Are you in a Growth Pivot?

Planning a Reinvention?

Navigating a Redirect?

Working through a Rebuild?

Second, adjust your expectations and tactics accordingly:

Growth Pivots often need stronger signals of readiness and visibility in your current lane.

Reinventions require learning, experimentation, and building credibility in a new space.

Redirects call for emotional processing and strategic re‑targeting of your existing strengths.

Rebuilds benefit from support, patience, and a focus on transferable skills and values.

Naming your transition type doesn’t solve everything. But it gives you a clearer map of what you’re navigating—and that makes it easier to choose the right next steps.
