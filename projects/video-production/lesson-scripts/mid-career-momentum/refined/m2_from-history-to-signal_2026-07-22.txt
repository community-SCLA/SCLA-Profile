By mid-career, your resume and your professional profiles have quietly turned into archives — a long record of everything you have ever done. That makes it harder for anyone to see where you are heading next, and why you are ready for your next role.

So let's turn the history into a signal.

Start with what the other side is actually doing. When a manager, a sponsor, or a hiring committee looks at your materials, they are scanning for three things. What kinds of results you have delivered. The level of scope you have handled. And how well your experience lines up with the role you want next.

That is the whole read. Results, scope, fit for the next role.

Now look at your own bullets against that. If your resume reads like a task list — "responsible for X," "duties included Y" — they have to work much harder to see your impact. Compare "responsible for managing projects" to "led a cross-functional project that reduced onboarding time by thirty percent." Same work. One of them shows the impact.

The same thing happens on LinkedIn. If your headline describes only your current role, the people who could open a door for you may never realize you are aiming at a different kind of future.

In this module, we will make three shifts together.

First, discovering the experiences that best support your future trajectory.

Second, rewriting your key resume bullets to show results, scope, and impact.

Third, updating your LinkedIn headline and summary so they speak to your future self, not just your past self.

The goal is not to rewrite your entire history. It is to curate and highlight the parts of your story that make your Next Move Statement believable and compelling. When you reposition your experience, you make it easier for decision-makers to connect the dots from who you are now to who you are becoming.

So keep one question in mind as you work through this module. If someone only saw my resume and my LinkedIn profile, would they understand the future I am aiming for?

By the end, you will be much closer to a yes.
