At this point in your career, most meaningful opportunities do not come from job boards. They come from people — colleagues, leaders, and connectors who know your work and can open doors.

In this module we are focusing on three kinds of relationships that matter most during a mid-career pivot or a push for advancement: peers, sponsors, and opportunity holders.

Peers are professionals at a similar level who share knowledge, introductions, and real-world context. They help you understand what is actually happening on the ground, compare experiences, and build community while you shift direction. Your peers are colleagues in your department, practitioners in the field you are moving toward, and members of the professional communities you belong to.

Sponsors are more senior leaders who do not only advise you — they advocate for you. They put your name forward for roles, projects, and committees. Sponsorship matters enormously for mid-career advancement and for pivots, because sponsors often sit where decisions are made.

Opportunity holders are the people who control or influence roles, projects, and hiring decisions. Hiring managers. Program directors. Deans. HR and talent partners. Leaders at organizations you would like to join. Even if they do not know you yet, they are key nodes on your future path.

When you are pivoting into a new field or aiming at a higher-level role, your goal is not to meet everyone. Your goal is a small, deliberate map of the people most relevant to the future you are building.

So how do you find them?

Start with the connections you already have. Current and former colleagues. People you have worked with on cross-functional projects. Alumni networks, professional associations, and the communities you are already part of. Let them know about your Next Move Statement, and ask: who do you know in this field or role that I might talk to for a twenty-minute learning conversation?

Second, search directly in your future field. Use LinkedIn and professional groups to search by title, by skill, or by keywords tied to the roles you want. Look for people in the organizations and teams you would like to join.

Third, identify potential sponsors, whether in your current organization or the one you are heading toward. Look one to three levels above you, at people who already appreciate your work or seem aligned with the direction you are taking. And look at the leaders who are visible in the spaces you want to enter.

In this module's activity, you will build a contact map of three to five strategic relationships — peers, sponsors, and opportunity holders — who are relevant to your next move. Then you will use the outreach templates and AI to craft messages that still sound like you.

One thing worth being clear about: networking is not collecting business cards, and it is not asking strangers for favors at random. It is building real relationships, sharing something of value, and staying visible to the people most connected to the future you are creating.

As you go, keep one question in front of you. Who already sits near the opportunities I care about, and how do I begin a genuine, respectful relationship with them?
