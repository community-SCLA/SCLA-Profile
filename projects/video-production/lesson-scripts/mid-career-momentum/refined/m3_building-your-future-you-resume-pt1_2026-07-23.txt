Hi, and welcome to this lesson on your future-you resume.

By mid-career, most of us have resumes that have quietly turned into archives — long lists of everything we've ever done. On the surface that looks thorough. In practice, it often works against us.

So today, let's see your resume differently: not as a historical record, but as a strategic tool that tells the story of where you're going next, and why you're ready for it.

Let's start with why so many mid-career resumes don't do us justice. There are three usual culprits.

First, they're overloaded with tasks but light on impact. You'll see bullets like "Managed projects," "Supported faculty," "Handled student inquiries." Those describe activity, but they don't tell anyone what changed because you were in the role — no results, no scope, no value added.

Second, the format and language feel outdated or generic. We carry old sections forward and keep phrases we wrote a decade ago, never checking whether the document matches today's expectations. The result looks busy but not sharp or focused.

Third — and this is the big one for us — there's often no clear link between your experience and the role you want next. Someone can read what you've done, but not see how it connects to the step you want next. There's no through-line from "here's my history" to "here's why I'm ready for this."

All of that makes it harder for hiring managers, sponsors, and decision-makers to say: "Yes, this person is clearly the right fit, right now."

So let's flip the perspective. At this stage, your resume is less about proving you've been busy and more about showing you're ready for what's next. A future-you resume is built around the role you want, not the role you started in.

Instead of documenting everything, you're curating evidence — choosing the projects, results, and responsibilities that best support the direction you've already defined in this course. Every bullet answers one question: "How does this show I'm prepared for my next move?"

That might mean highlighting the initiatives where you led at a higher level than your title suggested. Emphasizing outcomes and scope — how many people you impacted, what improved. And de-emphasizing tasks or roles that no longer match where you're heading. Your resume becomes less of a timeline and more of a case file for your future you.

Earlier in this track, you created a Next Move Statement. You named your primary path — promotion, lateral move, or role redesign — and described the role you want in the next 6 to 12 months. In this lesson, that statement is your north star.

As you update your resume, you'll ask of every line: "Does this help make the case for the role in my Next Move Statement?" You'll choose three to five experiences that serve as your strongest evidence. And you'll rewrite them to clearly show responsibility, action, measurable outcome, and scope.

For example, instead of "Managed team projects," you might write: "Led a cross-functional team of 6 to streamline onboarding, reducing time-to-productivity by 30% and improving new-hire satisfaction scores by 20%." Same person, same work — but the second version paints a much clearer picture of your impact and your readiness for more.
