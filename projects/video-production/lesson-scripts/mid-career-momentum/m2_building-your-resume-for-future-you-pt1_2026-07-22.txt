Title slide / Opening

Hi, and welcome to this lesson on your future-you resume.

By mid-career, most of us have resumes that have quietly turned into archives—long lists of everything we've ever done, in every role we've ever held. On the surface, that looks thorough. But in practice, it often works against us.

Today, I want to help you see your resume differently: not as a historical record, but as a strategic tool that tells the story of where you're going next and why you're ready for it.

---

Segment 1: Why mid-career resumes often fail

Let's start with why many mid-career resumes don't do us justice.

First, they're overloaded with tasks, but light on impact.
You'll see bullet points like "Managed projects," "Supported faculty," or "Handled student inquiries." Those statements describe activity, but they don't tell anyone what changed because you were in the role. There's no sense of results, scope, or value added.

Second, the format and language can feel outdated or generic.
We carry old sections forward, keep phrases we wrote a decade ago, and never really revisit whether the document matches today's expectations. The result can be a resume that looks busy but doesn't feel sharp, modern, or focused.

Third, and most important for this course, there's often no clear link between your experience and your target role.
If someone picks up your resume, they might understand what you've done, but they may not be able to quickly see how it connects to the role you want next—whether that's a promotion, a lateral move, or a redesigned version of your current role. There's no through-line from "here's my history" to "here's why I'm ready for this specific next step."

All of that makes it harder for hiring managers, sponsors, and decision-makers to say: "Yes, this person is clearly the right fit for this role, right now."

---

Segment 2: Introducing the future-you resume

So let's flip the perspective.

At this stage, your resume is less about proving that you've been busy and more about showing that you're ready for what's next. A future-you resume is built around the role you want, not the role you started in.

Instead of trying to document everything, you're curating evidence.
You're choosing the projects, results, and responsibilities that best support the direction you've already defined in this course. Every bullet, every section, is there to answer a simple question: "How does this show that I'm prepared for my next move?"

That might mean:
* Highlighting the initiatives where you led at a higher level than your title suggests.
* Emphasizing outcomes and scope—how many people you impacted, what changed, what improved.
* De-emphasizing tasks or roles that no longer match where you're heading.

Your resume becomes less of a timeline and more of a case file for your future you.

---

Segment 3: Connecting to your Next Move Statement

Earlier in this track, you created a Next Move Statement. You named your primary path—promotion, lateral move, or role redesign—and described the kind of role you want in the next 6–12 months.

In this lesson, we'll use that statement as your north star.

As you update your resume:
* You'll ask: "Does this bullet help make the case for the role in my Next Move Statement?"
* You'll choose 3–5 experiences that serve as your strongest evidence for that role.
* You'll rewrite those experiences so they clearly show responsibility, action, measurable outcome, and scope.

For example, instead of "Managed team projects," you might say:
"Led a cross-functional team of 6 to streamline onboarding, reducing time-to-productivity by 30% and improving new-hire satisfaction scores by 20%."

Same person, same work—but the second version paints a much clearer picture of your impact and your readiness for more responsibility.
