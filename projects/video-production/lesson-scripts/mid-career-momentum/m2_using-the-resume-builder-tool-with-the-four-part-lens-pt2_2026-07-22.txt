Lesson: Mod 2 — Reposition Your Experience (vid 2, resume pair)
Title: Using the Resume Builder Tool with the Four-Part Lens

Welcome back. In the last video, we talked about the idea of a future-you resume—a resume that's built around the role you want next, not just everything you've done so far.

In this video, I'll show you how to actually do that using two things together: our Resume Builder Tool and a simple four-part lens for rewriting your bullets. By the end, you'll see exactly how to turn a vague task like "managed orientation sessions" into a clear impact statement that supports your Next Move Statement.

---

Segment 1: Reintroducing the Four-Part Lens

Let's start with the lens itself.

Any strong mid-career bullet can usually answer four questions:
* Responsibility — What were you expected to do?
* Action — What did you actually do?
* Measurable outcome — What changed as a result?
* Scope — How big was the impact? Who or what was involved?

When we apply this lens, we move from:
* "I did tasks" → "I created results."
* "I had a job" → "I handled responsibility and scope."

This is exactly what helps managers, sponsors, and hiring committees see you as ready for your next role.

You don't need perfect numbers or formal KPIs for every bullet. Even approximate outcomes—like "reduced time," "improved satisfaction," or "expanded reach"—can make your impact clearer.

---

Segment 2: Walking Through the Resume Builder Tool

Now let's walk through how to use this lens inside our Resume Builder Tool, supported by AI.

On your screen, you'll see the tool's main upload or text area.

1. Upload or paste your resume bullets

Start by either uploading your current resume or copying and pasting the bullets you want to focus on into the tool.

For this example, we'll take a simple, real-world bullet:
"Managed orientation sessions for new hires."

This is a common kind of statement: it tells us what you did, but not much about what difference it made, how often you did it, or how many people it involved.

2. Run the bullet through the AI rewrite feature

Next, you'll use the AI rewrite option in the tool.

Behind the scenes, we're asking the AI to look for responsibility, action, measurable outcome, and scope.

Let's see what that might look like.

Before:
"Managed orientation sessions for new hires."

After (AI-assisted):
"Led quarterly orientation sessions for cohorts of 30–40 new hires, implementing a new feedback loop that increased post-orientation satisfaction scores by 18% and reduced onboarding confusion reports by 25%."

Notice what changed:
* We now know how often you ran the sessions (quarterly).
* We know the scope (cohorts of 30–40 new hires).
* We see action (implementing a new feedback loop).
* We see measurable outcomes (higher satisfaction, reduced confusion).

Same basic responsibility—but now it reads as leadership, process improvement, and clear impact.

---

Segment 3: Editing AI Output and Keeping It True to You

The AI rewrite is a starting point, not the final version.

After you get a rewritten bullet, you'll want to:

1. Check accuracy
* Do the frequency, numbers, or outcomes reflect reality?
* If you didn't run sessions quarterly, change that.
* If you don't have exact percentages, you can adjust the language:
  * "increased satisfaction scores" → "improved satisfaction scores based on post-orientation feedback."
* The goal is honest, credible impact—not exaggeration.

2. Align the tone with your voice and field
* Different fields use different language.
* For example, in higher education or non-profits, you might prefer:
  * "enhanced new-hire onboarding experience" rather than "crushed onboarding KPIs."
* Adjust phrases so they sound like you and match your industry norms.

3. Keep the four-part structure
* Make sure the bullet still clearly shows:
  * Responsibility (orientation sessions for new hires)
  * Action (you led, you implemented, you improved)
  * Outcome (what changed)
  * Scope (how many people, how often, what size)

If you're ever unsure, you can ask yourself:
"If someone only read this one bullet, would they understand what I did, how well I did it, and at what level of responsibility?"

If the answer is yes, you're in good shape.

---

Segment 4: Choosing Bullets Based on Your Path

The last piece is choosing which rewritten bullets to bring forward, based on your Next Move Statement.

Think about your three possible paths:

Promotion in your current lane
* Prioritize bullets that show leadership, scope, and strategic decisions.
* Look for examples where you took charge, influenced direction, or owned outcomes beyond your formal title.
* These bullets say: "I'm already operating at the next level."

Lateral move to a different role or department
* Highlight transferable skills and cross-functional projects.
* Emphasize collaboration across teams, working with diverse stakeholders, or moving between domains.
* These bullets say: "I can bring my skills into new contexts and succeed."

Redesign of your current role
* Focus on process improvements, role evolution, and innovation.
* Show where you changed how work gets done, introduced new tools or systems, or reshaped your responsibilities.
* These bullets say: "I've already started redesigning my role; now I'm formalizing that change."

As you look at the AI-assisted rewrites in the Resume Builder Tool, don't try to perfect everything at once. Start by selecting the 3–5 strongest bullets that:
* Align most clearly with your chosen path
* Reflect your true impact and strengths
* Help others see you as the future-you described in your Next Move Statement

Those will become your "headline stories" on your resume and profile.
