===== SCRIPT (verbatim from dashboard) =====

When people hear “career pivot” or “repositioning,” they often imagine starting completely over—new degree, new industry, new identity.

In reality, many successful career pivots are strategic repositionings of the experience you already have. You’re not erasing your past; you’re re‑telling it so it clearly points to the future you want.

[On screen: Title text “Reposition Your Career (Without Starting Over)” with a visual of a road that bends rather than breaks.]

Let’s talk about how to do that in a practical way, and look at a few examples of how other mid‑career professionals have repositioned themselves.

[Graphic: Three simple character icons labeled “Alex,” “Jordan,” and “Priya,” with their old titles underneath.]

Example 1: Alex – From Operations Manager to Strategy Leader

Alex spent ten years as an Operations Manager, making sure processes ran smoothly, teams hit their numbers, and projects stayed on track. On paper, Alex’s roles looked operational, not strategic. But in reality, Alex had led cross‑functional projects, redesigned systems, and influenced decisions.

[On screen: Alex’s “before” resume snippet with bullets like “Managed daily operations,” “Oversaw team workflows.”]

To reposition for a Strategy Leader role, Alex did three things:

Curated experiences that showed strategic thinking—like redesigning a workflow that saved time and money, or leading a project that changed how a department worked.

Rewrote bullets to emphasize decisions, outcomes, and scope—turning “managed operations” into “Led multi‑department initiative that reduced processing time by 25% and improved customer satisfaction scores.”

Updated LinkedIn to reflect the future: “Operations & Strategy Leader | Transforming complex systems into scalable, efficient processes.”

[Motion graphic: Alex’s “before” bullet morphs into “after” bullet; old LinkedIn headline morphs into future‑oriented headline with “Strategy” highlighted.]

Alex didn’t pretend to have a Strategy Director title. Instead, Alex highlighted the strategic work already inside those operations roles and began applying to roles with that focus.

Example 2: Jordan – From Teacher to Learning Experience Designer

Jordan spent years teaching in the classroom, designing lessons, assessments, and learning experiences. Jordan wanted to move into instructional design and learning technologies—roles that often use different language and sit in different departments.

[On screen: Jordan’s “before” profile: title “Teacher,” bullets like “Developed lesson plans,” “Graded assignments.”]

To reposition, Jordan:

Translated teaching tasks into design language: “Designed and implemented multi‑modal learning experiences for diverse learners,” “Developed assessment strategies to measure learning outcomes.”

Highlighted tools and methods used—like learning platforms, data analysis, and collaborative project design.

Updated LinkedIn headline to: “Learning Experience Designer | Translating classroom expertise into engaging digital and hybrid learning.”

[Graphic: Side‑by‑side comparison of “Teacher” vs. “Learning Experience Designer,” with common skills highlighted between them.]

Jordan didn’t erase the classroom. Instead, Jordan positioned classroom experience as proof of strong design, facilitation, and learner‑centered thinking—directly relevant to the new field.

Example 3: Priya – From Individual Contributor to People Manager

Priya had spent most of her career as a strong individual contributor—taking ownership of complex tasks, building relationships, and delivering results. Priya wanted to step into a first‑line leadership role.

[On screen: Priya’s “before” bullets highlighting personal contributions only.]

To reposition:

Priya identified experiences where she’d informally led others—coordinating project teams, mentoring colleagues, onboarding new hires.

She rewrote bullets to show leadership behaviors and outcomes: “Mentored 3 new analysts, accelerating their ramp‑up time by 30%,” “Coordinated cross‑team project, aligning stakeholders and delivering ahead of schedule.”

She updated her profile summary to: “Emerging people leader with a track record of mentoring, coordinating cross‑team projects, and growing others while delivering results.”

[Motion graphic: A “leader” icon appears over Priya’s updated bullets; her summary now includes phrases like “growing others” and “leading projects.”]

Across these examples, the pattern is the same:

Clarify your future direction. Know whether you’re aiming for a promotion, lateral move, or role redesign, and what types of roles you want to show up on your radar.

Curate your story. Select experiences that clearly connect your present to that future—projects, responsibilities, and results that already demonstrate the skills you’ll need.

Translate your language. Rewrite bullets and profile sections so they use the phrases of your target roles—strategy, design, leadership, analytics, systems, whatever fits your path.

Update your public signals. Align your resume, LinkedIn headline, summary, and even your “60‑second career story” with that future‑focused positioning.

[On screen: A four‑step diagram labeled “Clarify → Curate → Translate → Update,” with arrows forming a loop.]

Repositioning your career is not about being someone you’re not. It’s about revealing the through‑line in what you’ve already done—and telling that story in a way that makes your next move feel obvious to others.

As you move forward in this track, keep asking:
“How can I tell the true story of my experience so it clearly points to the roles I want next?”

That’s the heart of repositioning—and it’s one of the most powerful ways to open new doors without starting from scratch.
