===== SCRIPT (verbatim from dashboard) =====

At this point in your career, most meaningful opportunities don’t come from job boards. They come from people—colleagues, leaders, and connectors who know your work and can open doors.

[On screen: Title “Strategic Networking & Outreach” over an abstract network graphic with nodes and lines.]

In this module, we’ll focus on three kinds of relationships that matter especially during a mid‑career pivot or advancement: **peers, sponsors, and opportunity holders.**

[Graphic: Three labeled circles: “Peers,” “Sponsors,” “Opportunity Holders,” connected to a center node labeled “You.”]

**Peers** are professionals at a similar level who share knowledge, introductions, and real‑world context. They help you understand what’s happening on the ground, compare experiences, and build community as you shift direction.

[On screen: Examples around the “Peers” circle: “Colleagues in your department,” “Practitioners in your future field,” “Members of professional communities.”]

**Sponsors** are more senior leaders who don’t just advise you—they actively advocate for you. They put your name forward for roles, projects, and committees. Sponsorship is especially critical for mid‑career advancement and career pivots, because sponsors often sit where decisions are made

[Graphic: “Sponsor” circle with an arrow pointing to a box labeled “Visible Opportunities,” such as “Promotion,” “Project lead,” “Committee seat.”]

**Opportunity holders** are people who control or influence roles, projects, and hiring decisions. This might be hiring managers, program directors, deans, HR or talent partners, or leaders in organizations you’d like to join. Even if they don’t know you yet, they are key nodes for your future path.

[On screen: Icons for “Hiring Manager,” “Program Director,” “External Organization Leader.”]

When you’re pivoting into a new field or aiming for a higher‑level role, your goal is not to meet *everyone*. Your goal is to identify a **small, strategic map** of people who are most relevant to the future you’re building.

[Graphic: A simple “relationship map” with a handful of nodes labeled “Peer,” “Sponsor,” “Opportunity Holder,” not a dense cluster.]

So how do you find these people?

First, start with **your existing connections**:

- Current and former colleagues
- People you’ve worked with on cross‑functional projects
- Alumni networks, professional associations, or communities you’re part of.

[On screen: List of sources with soft highlight: “Existing colleagues,” “Alumni,” “Communities.”]

Let them know about your Next Move Statement and ask:
“Who do you know in [target field/role] that I might talk to for a 20‑minute learning conversation?”

Second, use **platforms like LinkedIn and professional groups** to search directly in your future field:

- Search by title, skills, or keywords connected to your target roles.
- Look for people in organizations or teams you’d like to join

[Motion graphic: LinkedIn search bar with keywords like “Learning Experience Designer,” “Director of Student Success,” “AI Program Manager.”]

Third, identify **potential sponsors** in your current organization or new one:

- People 1–3 levels above you who appreciate your work or seem aligned with your direction.
- Leaders who are visible in the spaces you want to enter.

[Graphic: Org chart snippet with a few nodes highlighted as “Potential Sponsors.”]

In this module’s activity, you’ll build a contact map of 3–5 strategic relationships—peers, sponsors, and opportunity holders—who are relevant to your next move, then use outreach templates and AI to craft authentic messages.

[On screen: Text “3–5 Strategic Relationships” with sublabels “Peer,” “Sponsor,” “Opportunity Holder.”]

Remember: networking is not about collecting business cards or asking for favors at random. It’s about **building real relationships**, sharing value, and staying visible to the people who are most connected to the future you’re creating.

As you move forward, ask yourself:
“Who already sits near the opportunities I care about—and how can I begin a genuine, respectful relationship with them?”
